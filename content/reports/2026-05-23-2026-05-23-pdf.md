## 7. Goldman Sachs Global Markets Analyst: Fed Balance Sheet Outlook

### Conclusion

The selection of Kevin Warsh as next Fed Chair increased market attention on balance sheet shrinkage, but Goldman thinks a meaningful reduction is unlikely. The Fed is expected to maintain the ample-reserves framework, allow reserves to drift modestly lower as a share of bank assets, and later resume trend-like growth to keep reserves stable.

### Key Data

- Since mid-2008, the Fed balance sheet grew by US$5.8tn, from 6.4% of GDP to about 21% today; the peak was near 36% in 2022.
- Fed UST holdings are about 14% of Treasuries outstanding, below the pre-GFC average of about 17%.
- Fed agency MBS holdings are about 22% of the agency MBS market.
- Goldman estimates equilibrium balance-sheet expansion need at about US$25bn/month: US$9bn/month for currency growth, US$11bn/month to keep pace with bank asset growth, and US$5bn/month for TGA growth.
- Goldman expects reserve management purchases to average US$5bn/month through 1Q27, taking reserves to about 11% of bank assets.
- After early 2027, Goldman expects RMPs to return to a trend-like US$25bn/month.
- Baseline implies UST purchases of US$20-25bn/month near term and US$35-40bn/month steady state after including MBS runoff reinvestment into USTs.

### Investment Implications

The note argues against expecting a large QT shock. Keeping the balance sheet stable would still allow reserves to decline as a share of bank assets. A return to aggressive runoff would be more disruptive, but Goldman thinks the hurdle is high because overshooting reserve scarcity can cause funding stress.

### Risks and Watchpoints

- Warsh and other officials may tolerate somewhat higher short-term funding-rate volatility to encourage banks to economize on reserves.
- Bank liquidity rule changes could reduce regulatory-induced reserve demand.
- Temporary non-reserve liabilities such as the TGA can create reserve volatility; better mitigation tools could allow a smaller buffer.
- Downside risk to Goldman’s balance-sheet path exists, but the note still sees meaningful shrinkage as unlikely.

No direct trade recommendation. The macro implication is positive for EM growth, especially countries with digital services, healthcare gaps, cloud adoption, and low-cost AI access. It also supports the idea that AI upside is not only a US mega-cap equity story.

### Risks and Watchpoints

- EM labor forces are more production-heavy and less exposed to white-collar automation than DM labor forces.
- Adoption surveys may be noisy and may underestimate major EM progress.
- The healthcare uplift is illustrative, not a base-case forecast.
