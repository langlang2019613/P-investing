## FOMC Minutes

English standalone summary
Source file: 1.USA_ FOMC Minutes Note "Almost All" Participants See the Committee "Well Positioned" at Current Policy Rate.pdf

Conclusion:
The March FOMC minutes show a Fed that is deliberately keeping policy two-sided. Almost all participants agreed that the 3.5%-3.75% funds-rate range leaves the Committee "well positioned" to respond as data evolve. The important change is not that the Fed is preparing to hike immediately, but that the easing bias is being diluted: many participants still think cuts will likely be appropriate in time if inflation cools, while some participants argued for language that explicitly recognizes the possibility of hikes if inflation remains above target.

Key data and arguments:
The minutes say a majority of participants judged the Middle East conflict had increased both upside risks to inflation and downside risks to employment. Most participants said further labor-market softening could warrant additional cuts, while many said higher oil-driven inflation could call for rate increases. The staff GDP forecast was weaker than at the January meeting, reflecting softer incoming data and tighter financial conditions, while the inflation forecast was slightly higher because of incoming price data and higher oil prices.

Labor-market commentary is important. Many participants described the labor market as a low-hire, low-fire environment that is vulnerable to adverse shocks. Several also noted firms may delay or reduce hiring in anticipation of AI adoption. This matters because it gives the Fed a reason to watch employment deterioration closely, but it does not override the inflation risk from energy.

Investment implications:
This note argues against assuming a simple cutting cycle. Front-end rates should remain sensitive to inflation surprises, oil prices, and labor-market data. Duration can rally if employment weakens or oil falls, but the Fed is not giving markets a clean dovish signal. Equity investors should treat the policy backdrop as supportive only if inflation data cooperate. High-duration growth stocks face a valuation headwind if the market prices out cuts or starts pricing hike risk.

Risks to the view:
A quick Middle East de-escalation and softer inflation could restore the case for cuts. Conversely, persistent oil-price pressure or higher inflation expectations could push the Fed toward an even more hawkish stance.

## China Industrials Middle East Tracker

English standalone summary
Source file: 10.China tracker_ Middle East crisis - What Chinese corporates are telling us about their operations_ Industrials sector(1).pdf

Conclusion:
The report surveys Chinese machinery, building materials, chemicals, and advanced-material companies on Middle East conflict exposure. The direct demand shock is still manageable, but cost, shipping, feedstock, and second-order stagflation risks are rising. The key conclusion is that the conflict is not yet causing widespread order cancellations, but it is already altering logistics costs, raw-material prices, and pricing behavior.

Key data and arguments:
In machinery, Middle East export exposure varies widely: concrete machinery has the highest exposure at about 35% of exports, while tractors are only around 3%. Companies reported no broad order cancellations so far, though one construction machinery company said regional conflict weighed on 1Q26 demand. The long-term demand story for Saudi Vision 2030 and UAE Centennial Plan remains intact, but prolonged conflict could delay customer decisions and project timelines. Shipping disruption and higher freight costs are the main operational problems. A construction machinery company said every 10% change in steel cost affects product gross margin by roughly 0.5 percentage point. Rubber and tire costs are also rising.

Building-material companies are more domestic China-oriented, so the issue is raw materials rather than end demand. Bitumen prices, tied to refinery byproducts, rose about 33% after the crisis, prompting waterproofing companies to raise ASPs by around 10%. TiO2 prices rose roughly 20% after the crisis, leading coating companies to raise prices by about 3%-15%. PE/PP/PVC prices rose 15%-45%, and pipe companies are passing through costs with a lag of about one month because of inventory coverage.

Chemical companies have Middle East export exposure of roughly 5%-18% in the covered products. The conflict has pushed up feedstock and raw-material costs. Some firms benefit in the near term because product prices rise before inventory costs fully reset, temporarily improving spreads. The main concern is olefins-chain feedstock availability, especially naphtha and LPG imports from the Middle East.

Investment implications:
The better-positioned companies are those with cost-plus mechanisms, FOB terms, overseas plants or assembly capacity, locked-in input prices, and the ability to raise ASPs. Electrification is a medium-term beneficiary because higher oil prices can accelerate adoption of electric machinery and LNG heavy-duty trucks. The worst exposure is commodity chemical companies reliant on Middle East feedstock without pass-through power.

Risks:
A prolonged war could shift the problem from costs to demand destruction. Shipping and insurance disruption could also widen margin dispersion between companies with local capacity and those dependent on China-to-Middle-East exports.

## Global Economic Indicators

English standalone summary
Source file: 10.Global_ GS Economic Indicators Update_ Higher Global Inflation in 2026(1).pdf

Conclusion:
Goldman's global indicator update shows a world economy still growing slightly above potential, but with inflation forecasts revised higher after the Middle East energy shock. The macro message is stagflation-lite rather than recession: activity indicators remain resilient, but headline inflation risk has returned and financial conditions have tightened modestly.

Key data and arguments:
Goldman's 2026 global headline inflation forecast has risen to 3.3% since the start of the war, while the global core inflation forecast has increased more moderately to 2.3%. The distinction matters: the immediate shock is energy-led, but the risk is that it leaks into core through transportation, goods, and expectations.

The global ex-Russia Financial Conditions Index tightened 2.2bp over the week, mainly through short rates. This is not a severe stress signal, but it shows monetary markets reacting to higher inflation risk. The Current Activity Indicator remains above potential: the global March CAI is +2.7% annualized versus Goldman's global potential estimate of 2.5%. Developed markets are at +1.9% versus potential of 1.6%, and emerging markets are at +3.8% versus potential of 4.2%. Within countries, the US CAI is +2.9%, China +4.4%, India +6.8%, Euro Area +0.6%, Germany -0.9%, Spain +2.8%, Japan +1.3%, and the UK +0.2%.

Forecast revisions show growth downgrades concentrated in economies exposed to the energy shock and trade channels, including Vietnam and the US in the near-term comparison. Inflation revisions are more widespread, with particularly large moves in countries exposed to food, fuel, or currency pressure.

Investment implications:
This report supports a cautious macro-risk stance. Risk assets can hold up because growth is not collapsing, but duration and rate-cut trades are vulnerable because inflation has moved in the wrong direction. Equities with pricing power, energy exposure, and AI-driven earnings are better positioned than rate-sensitive or low-margin sectors. EM countries with large energy-import exposure face more pressure.

Risks:
If energy flows normalize quickly, headline inflation forecasts could come down and financial conditions could ease. If the shock persists, the inflation revision could broaden into core and push central banks away from easing.

## US Consumer

English standalone summary
Source file: 11.Americas Consumer Cyclicals_ State of the US Consumer_ Lower pre-savings DCF for 2026 on higher energy prices.pdf

Conclusion:
Goldman lowers its 2026 discretionary cash flow outlook for US households because higher oil, energy, and food costs are absorbing income gains. The aggregate consumer is not breaking, but the low-income consumer is now much more pressured. The report's most important conclusion is that higher essential spending nearly offsets the benefit from tax cuts and lower interest rates.

Key data and arguments:
Goldman lowers 2026 discretionary cash inflow growth to +4.2%, down from +5.1% in January, and only slightly above +4.1% in 2025. Disposable personal income growth is trimmed to +5.0% from +5.2%, still above the 2009-2019 average of 4.0%. Total household cash inflow is expected to grow +4.6%, helped by expected mortgage equity withdrawal growth of +7.8% and borrowing growth of +10.6%. Financial obligations are expected to ease slightly to 14.3% of DPI from 14.5% in 2025.

The negative surprise is essential spending. Goldman now models essential expenditures growing +7.4% in 2026, versus +2.4% previously and +4.3% in 2025. Energy goods and services spending is expected to grow +12.3%, compared with -4.2% previously, and food spending +6.6%, compared with +3.9% previously. The savings-rate assumption is cut to 4.5% of DPI from 5.6%, which keeps adjusted DCF growth at +4.2%, but this means consumers are using lower savings to defend spending.

The distributional impact is severe. For the bottom income quintile, higher energy and food costs plus SNAP and Medicaid cuts reduce pre-savings DCF growth to just +0.8% in 2026, down from +2.4% in 2025 and much lower than the aggregate +4.2%. Goldman estimates a roughly 50bp aggregate discretionary-spending-power headwind from $100 oil, but about 135bp for the bottom quintile.

Investment implications:
Best-positioned stocks listed by Goldman include DKS, WMT, ULTA, PM, MNST, PEP, STZ, TPR, TJX, ROST, ATZ, ANF, GRGD, CCL, MAR, HLT, DPZ, MCD, YUM, HSY, KR, SFM, and TSN. The theme is quality consumer exposure, value retail, staples, restaurants with scale, and travel/leisure where demand remains resilient. The riskier areas are discretionary categories dependent on low-income spending.

Risks:
A lower oil price would restore spending power. A more persistent oil shock or labor weakness would pressure the bottom quintile further and could hit discretionary retail revisions.

Goldman maintains Buy on copper and gold names, remains Neutral on Hongqiao, and upgrades Chalco-H/A to Neutral. The most attractive exposure is companies with metal price leverage plus volume growth, especially Zijin and CMOC. Aluminium has improved but is more margin/spread sensitive. Risks:
The main risks are metal price reversal, cost inflation, weaker output, policy intervention, and valuation after strong commodity-stock performance.

## North America Pipelines

English standalone summary

Source file: 13.Americas Pipelines and MLPs_ Basin Infrastructure and Production Dynamics.pdf

Conclusion: Goldman expects only a moderate US production response despite the commodity rally. The report’s central conclusion is that North American energy infrastructure growth will be concentrated in gas and NGLs rather than crude oil, with the Permian, Canada, Marcellus/Utica, and Haynesville as the most important basins to monitor. Most basins appear unconstrained later in the decade, but Canada crude egress and Marcellus/Utica gas takeaway remain key pinch points. Permian gas also tightens because of higher gas-oil ratios.

Key data and infrastructure details: The report expects high gas growth, moderate NGL growth, and low oil growth. Major projects include Desert Southwest at 2.3 bcf/d by 4Q29; Eiger Express up to 3.7 bcf/d in 2H28; Tallgrass expansion of +1.5 bcf/d in 4Q29; GCX in 2Q26; Blackcomb in 3Q26; Hugh Brinson in 4Q26/1Q27; Haynesville NG3 in 4Q25; Cloud Connector +2 bcf/d in 2Q29; WBI Bakken East +1 bcf/d around late 2030; Speedway +500 kb/d Permian NGL in 3Q27; Bahia +400 kb/d in 4Q27; and multiple Canada-related projects including Mainline, TMX, Express Platte, and MLO2.

Investment advice: Goldman’s framework is constructive for pipeline and midstream companies tied to Permian gas, NGLs, and Canadian egress. TRGP and KGS are notable beneficiaries because rising Permian GOR supports NGL and gas infrastructure needs. The report also implies that producer discipline limits the risk of an uncontrolled oil-supply response. Key risks are faster-than-expected producer activity, project delays, regulatory friction, and demand changes from LNG, power, or industrial users.

Source file: 13.China Property Weekly Wrap_ High frequency data, policy updates, and developer valuation.pdf

Conclusion: China property activity showed a sequential pause during week 14 because of the Qingming holiday, but year-on-year comparisons improved on an easy base. The report does not argue for a structural recovery yet. Instead, it presents a market supported by policy easing and secondary-market resilience, while primary sales, new starts, and completions remain weak. ## China Consumer Durables Middle East

English standalone summary

Source file: 16.China Tracker_ Middle East crisis - What Chinese corporates are telling us about their operations_ Consumer Durables(1).pdf

Conclusion: Goldman’s tracker suggests Chinese consumer durables companies have limited direct revenue exposure to the Middle East, but the conflict still matters through freight costs, shipment timing, petrochemical inputs, and margins. The direct demand impact is generally small because most surveyed companies report negligible to low-single-digit Middle East revenue exposure. However, companies with overseas sales above 30% of total revenue still face operational risk if logistics disruption and input inflation persist.

Key operational findings: The surveyed companies generally report normal production and no severe shortage of raw materials so far. Some appliance shipments have been disrupted, but management teams describe the total impact as controllable. The reason is that many exporters use long-term shipping contracts or FOB terms, which reduce direct exposure to volatile spot freight. Even so, companies warn that prolonged conflict could raise shipping costs and create margin pressure, especially if customers ask for delivery delays or renegotiation.

Cost structure and data: Petrochemical-linked inputs are the most important indirect channel. Companies repeatedly mention plastics, MDI/TDI, refrigerants, TDI, and PU as key raw materials exposed to energy and chemical-price inflation. Some companies have already raised prices, while others may adjust promotions or pricing if cost inflation continues. The report therefore treats the Middle East shock more as a margin and working-capital risk than a direct revenue shock.

Investment advice: Prefer consumer-durables exporters with diversified overseas markets, low Middle East dependence, FOB terms, pricing power, and lower petrochemical intensity. Companies with high plastic/chemical input exposure and weaker pricing power are more vulnerable. Main risks are longer conflict duration, client renegotiation, spot-freight spikes, petrochemical inflation, and demand weakness caused by higher energy prices.

## Oil Tracker Hormuz

English standalone summary

Source file: 2.Oil Tracker_ Hormuz Flows Edge Up, Asian Products Still Under Pressure.pdf

Conclusion: Goldman’s oil tracker argues that physical oil markets remain extremely tight despite modestly higher flows through the Strait of Hormuz. Nearby Brent and WTI futures rose $7/bbl and $15/bbl over the prior week as hopes for immediate peace faded. The report emphasizes that paper futures do not fully capture the stress in physical product markets, especially in Asia and Europe, where refined-product prices reached extreme levels.

Key geopolitical details: Iran reportedly rejected ceasefire talks. The US struck 50 military targets on Kharg Island while avoiding energy infrastructure. Attacks on UAE and Kuwait refining and petrochemical plants resumed. These events kept the market focused on both crude-flow disruption and downstream product shortages.

Key data: Hormuz exports edged up to 2.0 mb/d, but that was still only 10% of normal on a four-day moving average. Persian Gulf flows rose to 8.6 mb/d, about 37% of normal. ## Technology Disruption Workers

English standalone summary

Source file: 9.US Economics Analyst_ The Long-Run Effects of Technological Disruption on Workers.pdf

Conclusion: Goldman’s economics analysis finds that historical technology displacement creates lasting worker scarring. This matters for AI because even if AI raises aggregate productivity, workers displaced from technology-exposed occupations may suffer persistent earnings, employment, and wealth losses. The report uses more than four decades of individual-level National Longitudinal Survey data covering 20,000+ people born in the 1950s-60s and 1980s, combined with technology-disrupted occupations since 1980.

Key labor-market findings: Displaced workers from technology-disrupted occupations take about one month longer to find a new job and suffer more than 3% real earnings loss upon reemployment. By contrast, losses for other displaced workers are negligible. The mechanism is occupational downgrading: displaced workers often move into more routine, less analytical, and less interpersonal jobs.

Long-term scarring: Over ten years, real earnings for technology-displaced workers grow nearly 10 percentage points less than for never-displaced workers and 5 percentage points less than for other displaced workers. Repeat unemployment risk stays higher. Early-career displacement slows wealth accumulation and delays homeownership and household formation. This means the costs are not only short-term wage losses but also balance-sheet and life-cycle effects.

Who is less harmed: Young, college-educated, urban workers have cumulative losses about half as large. Short-tenure workers also fare better. Retraining helps when it moves workers toward more analytical, ICT-complementary jobs.

Macro context: Recessions amplify costs by adding about three weeks of unemployment and a five percentage point higher probability of later unemployment or labor-force exit. Goldman previously estimated AI could displace 6-7% of US workers over a decade and lift unemployment by up to 0.5 percentage points during transition.

Investment advice: AI productivity upside has real policy and social costs. Investors should monitor retraining, labor-market flexibility, and companies that use AI to augment skilled labor rather than simply eliminate entry-level pipelines.

## ChatGPT Agentic Pivot

English standalone summary

Source file: MS_What_Does_ChatGPT's_Agentic_Pivot_Mean_for_the_Ecosystem_加水印.pdf

Duplicate note: The selected file MS_What_Does_ChatGPT's_Agentic_Pivot_Mean_for_the_Ecosystem.en.zh-CN_加水印.pdf appears to be a translated/watermarked duplicate of the same Morgan Stanley report, so both selected files are merged into this single draft.

Conclusion: Morgan Stanley interprets ChatGPT’s agentic shopping pivot from direct checkout to link-out toward retailer and supplier apps as positive for adoption, but also as evidence that broad horizontal agents have not yet broken existing shopping behavior. The report’s central argument is that agentic commerce is real, but the first durable winners may be retailers, OTAs, search platforms, and vertical agents rather than a single horizontal AI assistant owning the entire transaction.

Five main implications: First, the pivot was needed because commercial adoption stalled and ChatGPT shopping lacked deep selection, reviews, pricing, inventory, shipping, and post-purchase capabilities. Second, adoption monitoring and the possibility of an Amazon partnership are key. Third, Google’s search moat remains strong, and Google still controls the pace of full agentic commerce more than the market assumes. Fourth, vertical-specific agents are likely to show value before horizontal agents because they have richer domain data and clearer transaction workflows. Fifth, retailers and OTAs may benefit if ChatGPT sends traffic at lower customer-acquisition cost than paid search.

Key data: Around 55% of ChatGPT users say they use it for product research or price comparison, and about 40% say they purchase, but these metrics have not meaningfully grown for five months. In the US survey, 46% of respondents used ChatGPT in the past month, 32% used Gemini, 19% Meta AI, 14% Copilot, 4% Perplexity, 14% Amazon Rufus, 13% Walmart Sparky, and 8% Target’s assistant. Among non-users, 34% prefer retailer sites/apps, and 36% do not trust AI shopping recommendations. Google remains dominant: about 56% first go to Google for product research, and 40-41% use it for price comparison, while ChatGPT remains small. Amazon Rufus has added roughly 140-200bp to GMV growth.

Investment advice: Beneficiaries include BKNG, AMZN, DASH, WMT, and EBAY; disintermediation fears for ETSY may ease. ChatGPT’s commission is described as single-digit, versus effective search take rates 5-10x higher. Morgan Stanley rates GOOGL Overweight with a $330 price target, $415 bull case, and $200 bear case. Risks are faster direct AI checkout, rising trust in AI recommendations, commission changes, and retailer pushback.
