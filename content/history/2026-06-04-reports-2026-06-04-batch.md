## BofA Global Fund Manager Survey

Source: BofA Global Fund Manager Survey, "In It to Win It" (19 May 2026). The English and Chinese-translated copies were merged into this one draft.

Executive conclusion: This survey shows a near-complete capitulation by formerly cautious investors into risk assets. BofA's bottom line is that the May survey recorded a record monthly rise in equity allocation and a sharp fall in cash, powered by stronger EPS optimism and expectations for Fed cuts. That is bullish in momentum terms, but tactically dangerous: BofA's Bull & Bear Indicator is now 7.8, close to a sell signal, and the cash level fell from 4.3% to 3.9%, a level BofA treats as a sell signal. BofA therefore argues early June is ripe for profit-taking, with the scale of any pullback likely determined by long-end bond yields.

Key data and evidence: The survey ran May 8-14, 2026. 200 panelists with $517bn AUM participated; 170 investors with $461bn answered global questions and 92 with $209bn answered regional questions. Macro fear has melted: only 4% expect a hard landing, 46% expect a soft landing, and 39% expect no landing. EPS optimism jumped sharply, with a record increase in investors expecting double-digit earnings growth. Iran/Hormuz anxiety is present but not dominant: 66% expect the Hormuz bottleneck to end in the next few months.

Risk positioning is crowded. "Long global semiconductors" is viewed as the most crowded trade by 73% of respondents. Inflation is the top tail risk at 40%. The most likely sources of a credit event are shadow banking at 42% and AI hyperscalers at 34%. Asset allocation is aggressively risk-on: investors are the most overweight cyclicals versus defensives since January 2018, the most overweight tech since February 2024, hold the fourth-highest commodity overweight in the survey history, and are the most underweight bonds since June 2022. Eurozone moved to underweight for the first time since December 2024, while consumer stocks are highly out of favor.

Investment advice / trade implications: This is not a clean bearish signal, but it is a warning against chasing crowded AI/semiconductor momentum without hedges. BofA's contrarian framework suggests covering shorts in bonds, the US dollar, UK assets and consumer stocks, while paring length in commodities, equities, EM assets and tech/semis. The major macro risk is a behind-the-curve Fed: only 16% expect Fed hikes in 2026, while 62% target a 6% 30-year Treasury yield versus only 20% targeting 4%. If the long end rises, equity FOMO can reverse quickly. Best action: keep core AI exposure but reduce crowded beta, hedge rates, and prepare to buy quality assets after volatility rather than adding into the most extended positioning.

## China AI Short Drama Industry

Source: Caixin Weekly preview, "Live-action short dramas shrink sharply; AI dramas fit low-quality traffic-running better" (30 May 2026).

Executive conclusion: The article describes a rapid structural shift in China's micro-drama industry from labor-intensive live-action production toward AI-generated short dramas and AI comics. The biggest strategic winner is ByteDance, because it controls nearly every important layer of the value chain: IP sources through Tomato Novel, model/tooling through Doubao/Jimeng/Seedance, cloud and MaaS through Volcano Engine, distribution through Hongguo and Douyin, and paid traffic through Ocean Engine. The industry is not just adopting AI as a tool; value capture is migrating away from actors, writers, local production bases and mid-tier studios toward model providers, cloud compute, traffic platforms and IP owners.

Key data: Doubao large-model daily token usage exceeded 120 trillion by March 2026, doubling in three months. IDC says Volcano Engine held 49.5% of China's enterprise MaaS call volume in 2025, versus Alibaba Cloud at 28%. Tomato Novel reached 260mn MAU in February. Hongguo Free Short Drama and Hongguo Free Comics were both the largest in their categories, with March 2026 MAU of 315mn and 25.63mn respectively. ByteDance's 2026 AI infrastructure investment plan is described as an unprecedented $70bn. In contrast, the live-action industry had been supported by subsidies and guarantees: Hongguo's 2025 guaranteed payments totaled Rmb2.5bn, with many projects receiving Rmb200k-350k each, and nearly 10,000 short dramas went online per quarter.

The cost shock is the heart of the story. A live-action short drama generally required about 40 people, one week of preparation, 3-5 days of filming, and Rmb300k-400k or more in production cost. An AI short drama can be made with fewer than 10 people, under Rmb100k, and in some cases below Rmb20k. This cost compression caused many production companies to stop live-action projects immediately after Hongguo cancelled guaranteed cooperation with mid-tier live-action studios in late February and shifted toward pure revenue share.

Important nuance: AI is not yet superior in every metric. During the 2026 Spring Festival season, live-action releases were only 1/50 of AI drama output, yet total live-action views were still 25 times AI drama views. This means AI dominates supply, not necessarily user preference. But AI content is especially compatible with low-cost volume testing, traffic arbitrage and algorithmic distribution. AI comics are growing explosively: Hongguo Free Comics MAU rose from 2.89mn in November 2025 to 25.63mn in March 2026, a monthly compound growth rate near 73%, while comic production capacity increased more than 80% by May 2026.

Investment / strategic advice: The investable logic favors platform and infrastructure layers over traditional studios. ByteDance's closed-loop ecosystem is the most obvious strategic winner. Upstream AI compute, video generation tools, cloud MaaS, IP libraries, ad-tech and distribution platforms should capture more economics than local production bases in Zhengzhou/Xi'an. However, low-quality AI content is already facing regulatory and platform cleanup: from April 7-15, Hongguo intercepted or punished 3,522 low-quality/violating AI comics, and it raised review standards again in May. The medium-term winners will not be pure content factories, but players that combine IP, model capability, traffic efficiency, compliance, and overseas distribution.

## China Credit Card Downturn

Source: Caixin Weekly preview, "Rising NPLs and competition erode the business: how to save bank credit cards?" (30 May 2026).

Executive conclusion: China's credit-card business has moved from growth to structural contraction. The old model of high card count, high benefits and high credit limits no longer works in a mobile-payment and embedded-consumer-credit world. Card issuers face simultaneous pressure from falling card volumes, shrinking distribution networks, deteriorating asset quality, declining revenue, and regulatory tightening. The industry can recover only by shifting from pure payment/card issuance toward disciplined consumer-finance use cases, unified credit-risk standards, and higher-value installment scenarios such as home improvement, autos and education.

Key data: By end-March 2026, credit cards and debit-credit combined cards in use totaled 687mn, down 1.3% quarter-on-quarter and down for 14 consecutive quarters. Versus the September 2022 peak of 807mn cards, the system has lost roughly 120mn cards in three and a half years. Many large and mid-sized banks reported 2025 declines in credit-card loan balances and transaction volumes. Credit-card income at all listed banks fell year-on-year in 2025 for the first time; state-owned banks generally saw revenue declines above 10%, while joint-stock banks fell 15%-20%, mainly due to falling loan scale, fee concessions and credit impairment.

The operating network is also shrinking. Bank of China announced that its credit-card app "Colorful Life" would stop service on June 30, 2026 and migrate functions into mobile banking. Multiple banks have closed or merged offline credit-card branches; incomplete statistics show at least 60 credit-card sub-centers ceased operations in 2025. Most credit-card apps lack active users, except a few leaders such as CMB's "Pocket Life," which still had 40.72mn MAU in 2025.

The customer problem is generational. Younger consumers aged 18-28 have weak understanding of credit-card features such as interest-free periods and grace periods, and prefer non-bank payment platforms with lightweight workflows, IP co-branding, digital benefits and social engagement. The core credit-card value group may now be age 29-49. Meanwhile, platforms such as Taobao, JD, Meituan and Douyin embed small-ticket, high-frequency consumer credit at the point of purchase, stealing mindshare and transactions.

Policy context: In April 2026, the PBOC and eight ministries issued the Financial Products Online Marketing Measures, effective September 30, 2026. The rule aims to separate payment, lending and e-commerce, and says non-bank payment institutions may not list loans or asset-management products as payment-tool options. This could reduce regulatory arbitrage and help bank credit cards, but enforcement matters because some platforms still push credit products at checkout or via "buy now pay later" paths.

Investment / strategic advice: This is negative for banks relying on card fees and broad unsecured consumer credit growth. It is relatively favorable for banks with stronger risk control, large active app ecosystems, lower funding costs and better ability to cross-sell qualified installment products. Auto installment is a bright spot but politically sensitive: Bank of Communications had Rmb117.6bn auto-installment balance in 2025, up 23.19%, with 1.0056mn customers, up 39.55%. Industry reform should focus on unified credit-limit management, stronger consumer-credit data sharing, and applying rigid credit deductions beyond cards to similar unsecured online lending products. Avoid business models dependent on card-count expansion; prefer disciplined consumer finance with scenario control and risk-adjusted profitability.

## Barron's June 1 2026

Source: Barron's U.S. Edition, 1 June 2026.

Executive conclusion: This Barron's issue is built around one dominant market question: the AI/chip rally looks bubble-like in breadth, but the best companies may still be undervalued relative to earnings growth. The issue separates durable AI infrastructure winners from speculative followers, while also warning retirees and diversified investors that S&P 500 concentration in technology and communications now creates portfolio-level AI risk. The practical message is not "sell all AI," but own quality AI infrastructure, avoid low-quality momentum, and rebalance portfolios that have become accidentally overexposed.

Key themes and data: The cover story argues that many chip stocks have become indiscriminately expensive, but five quality names have durable staying power: Nvidia, AMD, Broadcom, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing, and another memory/AI-related quality chip beneficiary highlighted in the article's table. Barron's notes that low-margin names such as On Semiconductor and STMicroelectronics had risen 122%, while Nvidia was up only about 30%, creating a valuation disconnect. The five favored chip names trade at two-year PEG ratios below 0.6x, versus the S&P 500 around 1.0x. Hyperscaler spending remains massive: Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet, Meta and Oracle could together spend more than three-quarters of a trillion dollars on AI data centers this year.

The Nvidia analysis is especially important. Nvidia EPS rose from $0.33 in FY2023 to $4.77 in FY2026, and the article cites expectations for EPS of $12.37 over the next two years, implying a P/E around 17x. Nvidia is not just GPUs; it also owns networking, CPUs, the CUDA software ecosystem and a growing investment portfolio. Its private-equity investments were valued at $43bn at the end of 1Q, up from $3.4bn at the start of 2025, and it holds stakes in Intel, CoreWeave and Synopsys. The risk is that customers want to diversify and inference opens a path for AMD, Broadcom/custom ASICs and hyperscaler chips. Barron's frames this as an opportunity for AMD and Broadcom rather than a death blow to Nvidia.

Other issue highlights: The contents flag consumer-trend beneficiaries, SLB's high-tech initiatives, Nike's uphill turnaround, AI risks to brokers' idle-cash profits, asset managers' exposure to buyout loans, and agriculture opportunities such as Corteva and Trimble. The retirement column warns that investors may be unintentionally overloaded in technology and communications because those sectors exceed 40% of the S&P 500. For retirees drawing income, concentration increases volatility. With the 10-year Treasury around 4.5%, rebalancing appreciated equities into bonds is presented as more attractive than before.

Investment advice: Keep exposure to the highest-quality AI infrastructure platforms, particularly those with durable moats, high margins and reasonable PEGs. Do not chase every semiconductor supplier just because it is attached to AI. Rebalance broad portfolios: hold no more than about half of the equity portfolio in S&P 500 trackers if concentration risk is a concern; consider value, low-volatility stocks, non-U.S. equities and bonds. Defensive low-AI-sensitivity names mentioned include Darling Ingredients, Kenvue, Johnson & Johnson, Coca-Cola and American Electric Power; the Invesco S&P 500 Low Volatility ETF is also discussed. The core portfolio lesson is barbell-like: quality AI leaders on one side, real diversification and income on the other.

## Lu Ting on AI and China K-Shaped Divergence

Source: Caixin, Lu Ting, "Dual K-shaped divergence under AI prosperity and weak property" (31 May 2026).

Executive conclusion: Lu Ting's argument is deliberately balanced. AI is a real technological revolution and China has become the clear No. 2 global AI center after the U.S., but the current AI boom is not large enough to solve China's macro problems by itself. Instead, AI may amplify two K-shaped divergences: divergence between high-end and low-end workers, and divergence between AI core cities and traditional lower-tier cities. Because AI benefits concentrate in Beijing, Shanghai, Shenzhen and Hangzhou, any property-market recovery is likely to be narrow and city-specific rather than national.

Key data: China's core AI industry exceeded Rmb1.2tn in 2025, around 0.9% of GDP, and could exceed 1% of GDP in 2026. AI-related fixed-asset investment is estimated to contribute 0.3ppt to 2026 GDP growth. Narrow AI infrastructure investment, mainly compute, semiconductors and data centers, could reach Rmb500bn in 2026. Broader AI capex including power-grid investment could reach Rmb1.2tn, or 0.8% of GDP. By comparison, U.S. AI capex is estimated at about 2.5% of U.S. GDP, meaning China's AI capex is roughly one-third of the U.S. level by GDP share.

The scale problem is central. Real estate investment once accounted for 14.5% of GDP in 2013 and still accounts for around 6% after five years of decline. Traditional infrastructure is about 14% of GDP. The 2022 peak of the "new three" industries - EVs, lithium batteries and solar panels - was around 2% of GDP. AI is strategically important, but currently too small to offset property weakness.

Export data show AI's power but also its limits. In April, AI-related goods contributed about half of China's 14.1% export growth: integrated circuits contributed 4.9ppts and automatic data-processing equipment 2.4ppts. But much of chip-export growth was price-driven rather than volume-driven: 1Q26 chip export value rose 77.5% while quantity rose only 13.4%; in April, integrated-circuit export value rose 99.6% while quantity rose only 3.7%. Thus nominal export strength exaggerates real GDP support.

The regional thesis: AI resources cluster around large models, top talent, compute and data. Beijing has more than 2,500 AI companies and the strongest model/chip design ecosystem; Shanghai is a high-end chip manufacturing and advanced packaging center; Hangzhou benefits from MaaS and AI+ industrial applications; Shenzhen dominates hardware, embodied AI and edge computing. Lower-tier cities may receive data-center or power infrastructure investment, but these are often lower-employment and lower-value-added nodes.

Investment / policy advice: For investors, the article supports exposure to China's hard-tech AI chain, power grid, storage, domestic chips and AI infrastructure, but cautions against extrapolating that into a broad China macro/property recovery. For policymakers, it recommends embracing AI while avoiding blind optimism, cleaning up real-estate debt more decisively, reforming local fiscal revenue after land-sale income has fallen more than 80% from the peak, improving social security, supporting lower-tier AI participation, and managing AI labor displacement. Lu Ting maintains a 2Q GDP growth forecast of 4.1%, materially below market expectations of 4.7%.

## Kiplinger's Retirement Report

Source: Kiplinger's Retirement Report, April 2026.

Executive conclusion: This issue is a personal-finance and retirement-planning package, not an equity research report. The core message is that traditional retirement planning is increasingly inadequate for two groups: families supporting adult children with disabilities and retirees facing extreme longevity. The lead article uses special-needs planning as a model for broader longevity planning: money may need to last through the retirees' lives and then support another dependent adult for 30-40 additional years. The practical takeaway is to structure assets, legal authority, benefits eligibility, insurance and successor decision-making before a crisis.

Key data and evidence: Nearly 1mn U.S. households have an adult with intellectual or developmental disabilities supported by an aging caregiver, according to Medicaid.gov. For many disability benefits, the individual generally must have less than $2,000 in their name, making direct asset ownership dangerous if it disqualifies Medicaid or Social Security support. The report also notes that U.S. centenarians are expected to quadruple by the 2050s to more than 400,000, up from about 2,300 in 1950. Even families without disabled children should plan for much longer time horizons than old rules of thumb assumed.

Planning structure: The report emphasizes special-needs trusts, which can preserve eligibility for government benefits while funding quality-of-life extras such as travel, recreation, therapies and better housing support. Trusts are also useful beyond disability planning: they can help with tax minimization, cash-flow planning, and intergenerational transfer. However, the article warns that trusts are not automatically the answer for every family. If most assets are retirement accounts, bank accounts and a residence, simpler beneficiary designations or account structures may be better than unnecessarily tying assets up in a trust.

Funding strategy: Families often combine a special-needs trust with life insurance and investment accounts. Insurance can provide a lump sum after parents die or can no longer provide care, while investments can support long-term inflation-adjusted needs. The article's example families are trying to answer very practical questions: who will oversee care, what is not covered by public programs, how to divide assets among siblings fairly, and how to ensure the disabled adult's medical and housing needs are met.

Other issue themes: The table of contents also includes hybrid target-date funds, gold and precious metals, Medicare trust-fund concerns, wealthy boomers, coffee and dementia research, longer life expectancy, collectibles valuation, and men's friendship loss in retirement. The editor's note flags strong reader reaction to Medicare Advantage and health-care funding cuts, showing health-care uncertainty remains a central retirement risk.

Advice: For any family with dependent adult children, create or update estate documents, special-needs trust, guardianship/supported-decision arrangements, trustee selection, insurance coverage and a written care roadmap. For ordinary retirees, treat longevity risk as a central risk, not a tail event. Maintain liquidity and simplicity where possible, but use trusts when eligibility preservation, control, tax planning or multi-generation care justify the complexity. The report's investment implication is conservative: plan for decades of spending, health shocks and inflation rather than relying on high returns to solve structural funding needs.

## Chinese EVs Enter Europe

Source: Caixin Weekly preview, "Chinese EVs enter Europe through multiple routes; the policy window is tightening" (30 May 2026).

Executive conclusion: Chinese automakers are no longer marginal in Europe. They are gaining share through multiple paths: exporting BEVs, switching into plug-in hybrids and ICE models to avoid BEV tariffs, using partners such as Stellantis, acquiring or restarting European factories, and localizing production. But the window is narrowing. EU tariff, local-content, investment-review and carbon rules are all moving toward higher barriers. The strategic advice is to accelerate localization before the Industrial Accelerator Act and related policies become binding.

Key data: ACEA data show China remained the EU's largest source of auto imports in 2025, with import volume up more than 30% year-on-year and exceeding 1mn units for the first time. China's own-brand BEV share in the EU BEV market rose from 2% in 2021 to 9% in 2025, while the EU BEV penetration rate rose from 5.4% to 17.4%. SAIC and BYD are the leading Chinese groups: SAIC's 2025 EU registrations rose more than 30% to 211,000, ranking 10th among EU automakers; BYD rose more than 200% to 128,000, ranking 14th. Their EU market shares were 1.9% and 1.2%; by the first four months of 2026 they had risen to 2.0% and 1.9%.

Market context: China sold over 13mn EVs in 2025, still the world's largest EV market, but growth slowed below 20%. Europe sold about 4.2mn EVs, up about 30%, and the U.S. sold about 1.5mn amid policy uncertainty. Europe is therefore the most attractive large overseas market for Chinese automakers. At the same time, European legacy automakers face pressure: S&P Global Mobility estimates major Western automakers' European capacity utilization fell from 79% in 2000-2019 to 68% in 2020-2025.

Company strategies: BYD initially focused on BEVs, but after the EU anti-subsidy tariff imposed in October 2024, it accelerated PHEVs. BYD faces a 17% EU anti-subsidy tariff on China-made BEVs. Its Seal U PHEV became Europe's best-selling PHEV in 2025 with more than 70,000 units; in Germany it starts at EUR39,900 versus nearly EUR50,000 for the VW Tiguan PHEV, about 20% cheaper. Leapmotor used Stellantis channels and saw EU registrations jump more than 500% in the first four months of 2026 to 28,000; in April alone it sold more than 8,000, with the T03 becoming Italy's top-selling BEV that month. Xpeng sold over 20,000 in Europe in 2025 and is testing VLA smart-driving systems, but European map and road complexity may limit replication of China capabilities.

Policy risks: The EU Industrial Accelerator Act draft requires EVs receiving public procurement or subsidies to be assembled in the EU, with 70% of vehicle cost excluding batteries sourced in the EU. It may also require pre-review for third-country players with more than 40% global manufacturing share investing over EUR100mn. Approval conditions include foreign ownership below 49%, EU employee share above 50%, annual EU R&D at least 1% of revenue, and at least 30% EU local procurement. The law may take effect in 2027-2028, making 2026 a window period.

Investment advice: Favor Chinese automakers with real localization, EU product fit, supply-chain compliance and after-sales networks: BYD, SAIC/MG, Chery, Xpeng and Leapmotor each have different paths. Avoid assuming every Chinese brand will win; more than 21 Chinese OEMs are in the EU, but only 9 sold more than 1,000 NEVs in 2025. Policy, carbon rules, service networks and product mismatch will separate durable global players from opportunistic exporters.

## The Lords of Easy Money

Source: Christopher Leonard, Chinese edition of "The Lords of Easy Money: How the Federal Reserve Broke the American Economy".

Executive conclusion: This book argues that post-2008 Federal Reserve policy, especially zero interest rates and quantitative easing, did much more than stabilize markets. It changed the distribution of wealth, pushed capital away from safe assets into riskier debt, inflated asset prices, encouraged corporate leverage, and created a fragile financial system dependent on continued Fed support. The book follows former Kansas City Fed president Thomas Hoenig as the dissenter who warned that monetary policy was not just about inflation, but about resource allocation and financial stability.

Core thesis: Zero rates create winners and losers. In a normal interest-rate world, banks, insurers and pension funds can earn returns from safe assets such as Treasuries. In a zero-rate world, safe yields disappear, forcing institutions out the risk curve. The Fed intended this risk-taking to restart the economy, but Leonard argues it also transferred money toward asset owners and Wall Street while encouraging speculation rather than productive investment.

Key mechanisms explained by the book: First, QE lowers Treasury yields by creating huge demand for bonds, pushing investors into stocks, corporate bonds, leveraged loans and structured credit. Second, the system becomes unstable because asset prices depend on Fed purchases; when Bernanke suggested slowing purchases in 2013, the "taper tantrum" showed how fast capital could move back toward safety. Ten-year Treasury yields jumped from around 2.2% before the press conference to 2.73% within weeks, and the Dow fell almost immediately after the announcement. The book treats this not as a random shock but as evidence that QE embedded fragility.

Corporate debt and CLOs are a major case study. Credit Suisse issued 11 CLOs worth $6.7bn from 2010 to 1H14 and became a major dealer. CLOs transformed complex leveraged loans into standardized products that pension funds, insurers, mutual funds and banks could buy by tranche: AAA, mezzanine or equity. This created steady demand for leveraged loans and allowed private-equity-backed companies to refinance repeatedly. Floating-rate loans looked safe to investors in a zero-rate world, but shifted risk to borrowers if rates rose.

The book highlights covenant-lite loans as a direct consequence of yield hunger. Sponsors increasingly demanded looser terms, and buyers accepted them because there was too much cash chasing too few yields. A $1bn loan could attract $2bn in demand. By 2014-2015, junk-bond analysts observed that markets had become detached from economic reality; negative information no longer mattered as long as QE liquidity kept demand strong. Global investment banking fees reached $11.1bn per month in June 2014, above the $10.7bn record set before the 2007 crisis.

Investment implications: The book is a warning against treating low rates as free. Easy money can support asset prices for years, but it also builds hidden leverage, poor underwriting and fragile funding chains. Investors should watch credit quality, covenant protection, floating-rate debt exposure, CLO demand, private-credit leverage and Fed liquidity dependence. It is especially relevant in an AI-capex cycle financed partly by debt and private markets. The core lesson is timeless: when policy suppresses safe yields, returns migrate to assets whose risks may be underpriced until liquidity reverses.

## China Wind Tender Data April 2026

Source: Caixin Data, "Wind Power Watch: April 2026 wind tender data rose sharply month-on-month and year-on-year" (1 June 2026).

Executive conclusion: China's wind-power tender pipeline rebounded sharply in April 2026, suggesting stronger future installation momentum. The near-term demand signal is positive for wind turbine, tower, electrical equipment, EPC and grid-related suppliers, but the report also makes clear that the real constraint is no longer only generation capacity. As wind/solar penetration rises, grid flexibility, storage, transmission channels and market mechanisms become the bottlenecks that decide how much new renewable capacity can be absorbed.

Key data: Caixin/Jiefeng's China Wind Capacity Index registered 140 in April 2026. There were 59 new wind-turbine-related events involving 23,524.93MW of capacity, up 263% month-on-month and 154% year-on-year. For historical context, the April index values from 2018 to 2025 were 94, 113, 91, 119, 136, 125, 128 and 127, respectively, making April 2026 meaningfully stronger than recent Aprils. Four single projects exceeded 500MW, mainly located in Altay, Xinjiang; Zhenglan Banner, Inner Mongolia; and Zhuolu County, Zhangjiakou, Hebei. Purchasers included China Energy Engineering, SPIC, China Huaneng and Shenzhen Energy North.

Winning-bid data show listed-company participation. In April 2026, 20 listed-company/subsidiary wind-capacity winning-bid events were recorded. The top three by number of projects were China Power, MCC and Mingyang Smart Energy, with 4, 2 and 2 projects respectively. Among disclosed wind-related winning bids, PowerChina had the largest single bid at Rmb1.17bn.

National power data support the structural story. By end-April 2026, China's total installed power capacity was 3.99bn kW, up 14.2% year-on-year. Wind installed capacity reached 660mn kW, up 22.0%. From January to April, average utilization hours of power-generation equipment were 925 hours, down 84 hours year-on-year, highlighting the consumption/utilization challenge.

Project filing data: In April, China added 5,692 registered new-energy power projects excluding residential distributed PV. Wind projects numbered 37, totaling 5,565.98MW. Liaoning had 6 projects totaling 1,085.2MW; the largest single project was a 1,000MW wind project in Ar Horqin Banner, Chifeng, Inner Mongolia.

Policy and investment implications: Central policy is pushing non-fossil energy, new storage, green power direct connection, smart microgrids and higher renewable consumption. The 15th Five-Year Plan emphasizes building a new power system. The National Energy Administration says it will support rational consumption of more than 200mn kW of annual new-energy additions by integrating "new energy + storage + grid + market." Key work includes offshore wind, desert/Gobi/wasteland bases, distributed wind/PV, pumped hydro, new storage, gas peakers, virtual power plants, vehicle-grid interaction, ultra-high-voltage transmission and electricity market reform.

Advice: The bullish signal is strongest for companies tied to large-scale wind tenders, grid upgrades, flexible resources, storage and renewable integration, not just turbine volume. Watch whether tender growth converts into installed capacity and whether utilization pressure worsens. Prefer firms with exposure to offshore wind, UHV/transmission, storage dispatch, grid automation and high-quality EPC. Avoid simple extrapolation from tender volume if local curtailment and pricing mechanisms do not improve.

## Tao Law and Semiconductor Beneficiaries

Source: Caixin Data, "Which sectors benefit from Huawei's Tao (tau) law?" (1 June 2026).

Executive conclusion: Huawei's "Tao/tau law" reframes semiconductor progress from geometric scaling to time scaling. Instead of defining progress only by smaller transistor nodes, the new framework optimizes system time constant tau across devices, circuits, chips and systems. The investable implication is that China's semiconductor opportunities should broaden from advanced nodes alone to advanced packaging, testing, storage, interconnect, EDA/IP, equipment, materials and system-level architecture. The market initially chased the whole semiconductor sector, but institutions see the strongest consensus around advanced packaging, testing and memory.

Market reaction and background: Huawei released the tau law on May 25. The CSI Chip Industry Index rose 6% that day, but pulled back in the following days. Advanced packaging outperformed: the Shenwan integrated-circuit packaging/testing index rose for two consecutive days with a 17% cumulative gain across 14 packaging/testing stocks. The reason is that tau law directly points to lower latency, higher bandwidth and shorter data paths, where packaging, chiplets, hybrid bonding and memory proximity matter.

Technical logic: Moore's law was a spatial-scaling framework: shrink transistor dimensions, increase transistor density and improve performance/cost. But after 2005, power scaling from geometry began to fail, and China also faces EUV lithography constraints. CITIC Securities argues that transistor miniaturization ultimately shortened signal transmission paths, so the deeper goal is time reduction. Tau law therefore uses "time miniaturization" rather than "geometric miniaturization" as the target, compressing signal propagation delay through innovations such as logic folding.

Industry data: The broader semiconductor cycle is being reaccelerated by AI. SIA data show global semiconductor sales reached $791.7bn in 2025, up 25.6%. In 1Q26, global sales reached $298.5bn, and March sales rose 79.2% year-on-year; China's March market rose 74.8%. SEMI expects global 300mm wafer-fab equipment spending to rise 18% in 2026 to $133bn and further to $151bn in 2027.

Beneficiary layers: At the device layer, focus shifts to channel mobility, transistor structures, contact resistance and local interconnect RC. At the circuit layer, vertical active-layer stacking, ultra-fine pitch hybrid bonding and critical-path folding reduce wiring and RC delay. At the chip layer, AI's data-movement bottleneck pushes logic and memory closer together; HBM, 3D-stacked SRAM and hybrid bonding become key. At the system layer, Unified Bus, Hi-ONE and 3D Folding lower data-transfer time across chips, racks and systems.

Investment advice: Institutions highlight three layers. First, architecture and design: AI chips increasingly need specialized design and software-hardware co-optimization. Second, advanced packaging and testing: bottlenecks increasingly occur between chips, chiplets, memory and compute. Third, equipment/materials/storage: AI training drives HBM, inference drives data-center storage, and both support long-term equipment demand. GF Securities specifically sees demand for bonding, thinning, etching, deposition, CMP and packaging inspection equipment. CITIC emphasizes hybrid bonding, TSV, multi-layer logic stacking, domestic fabs, electroplating, cleaning, CMP, etching, thin-film deposition, co-packaged optics and domestic advanced packaging.

Risk controls: The article warns that valuation can run ahead of fundamentals. Executive conclusion

This book is a long historical argument about energy as the foundation of civilization. Richard Rhodes does not present energy history as a simple sequence of technological triumphs. Instead, he shows that every energy system solves a previous bottleneck while creating new externalities, dependencies, and risks. Wood solved early heating and cooking needs but caused deforestation. Coal solved the wood shortage and enabled steam power but created mine disasters, urban pollution, and carbon emissions. Petroleum enabled mobility, aviation, mass logistics, and modern agriculture, but produced geopolitical dependency and climate damage. Electricity transformed industry and household life but required large centralized systems. Nuclear power offered extraordinary energy density but brought fear, waste, accidents, and proliferation concerns. Renewables reduce fuel pollution but need grids, storage, land, materials, and reliability management. The book's core conclusion is that the future energy challenge is not just discovering another fuel. It is designing an energy system that can support roughly ten billion people while reducing pollution, climate risk, and geopolitical fragility.

Main structure and arguments

1. Energy transitions begin with scarcity. Early societies depended heavily on wood for heat, cooking, metallurgy, building, and shipbuilding. As populations and urban needs grew, wood became insufficient. The book uses early modern Europe, including Elizabethan England, to show how wood scarcity pushed society toward coal. Coal was not adopted because it was pleasant; it was dirty and difficult. It was adopted because the alternative energy base could no longer support population and production needs.

2. Coal and steam changed the scale of civilization. Coal was first a heating fuel, but its deeper significance came when mining, steam engines, metallurgy, transport, and factories became connected. Mine flooding and ventilation problems created demand for mechanical pumping. Papin's pressure cooker and safety valve, Newcomen's atmospheric engine, and Watt's improvements were part of the same historical chain: energy scarcity created engineering problems, and those engineering problems produced the industrial revolution. Steam was not merely a machine; it was a new way to convert fossil energy into work independent of animal muscle, wind, or water location.

3. Lighting is treated as a major energy revolution. The book pays attention to whale oil, manufactured gas, kerosene, and electricity because the ability to light the night changed work, education, safety, commerce, and domestic life. This is one of the book's important insights: energy history is not only about factories and armies. It is also about daily life, hours of productivity, indoor air quality, and the expansion of human possibility.

4. Petroleum created modern mobility and geopolitical dependence. Oil's energy density made automobiles, trucks, ships, aircraft, and global logistics possible. It also transformed agriculture through mechanization and petrochemical inputs. But the same energy density made oil strategically sensitive. Control of production, refining, shipping lanes, and reserves became central to international politics. Petroleum gave societies unprecedented mobility while making them vulnerable to supply shocks and wars.

5. Electricity is a system, not simply a product. The book shows how electricity required generators, grids, motors, regulation, and large capital investment. Its value came from flexibility: the same network could power light, factories, appliances, communications, and later computing. This matters for the current era because AI, data centers, electric vehicles, and electrified industry are again making the power grid the center of economic strategy.

6. Nuclear power is portrayed as both a miracle of energy density and a social-political challenge. The technology can produce massive reliable power with low direct carbon emissions, but public fear, accidents, waste management, and weapons links have constrained deployment. The book does not treat nuclear as a simple answer, but it does make clear that excluding it entirely makes decarbonization harder.

7. Climate change is the final externality of the fossil-energy age. The book argues that the great benefits of coal, oil, and gas came with accumulated atmospheric costs. The problem is now global and time-delayed: past energy use creates current and future warming, and fast-growing societies still need affordable power.

Data and historical significance

The book's broad quantitative frame is that by 2100 the world may need to support around ten billion people, with much of the demand growth linked to large countries such as China and India. The essential question is not whether humanity can produce enough energy. Historically, humans have repeatedly found ways to unlock more energy. The harder question is whether the planet, atmosphere, cities, and political systems can absorb the waste and risk from how that energy is produced.

Investment and strategic implications

The best investment lesson from the book is to evaluate energy transitions through reliability, density, infrastructure, affordability, and externalities. Ideological narratives are insufficient. The winners in energy transitions are usually companies or systems that solve bottlenecks: mining pumps in the coal age, refining and engines in the oil age, grids and turbines in the electricity age, and today likely storage, transmission, advanced nuclear, gas flexibility, grid software, power equipment, critical minerals, carbon management, and industrial electrification. The book suggests that the next successful energy businesses will not simply produce cleaner energy; they will make clean energy reliable, financeable, scalable, and compatible with existing infrastructure.

## Japan Portfolio Strategy Shareholder Benefits

Source file: 7.JAPAN PORTFOLIO STRATEGY Unequal equities - How do stocks with shareholder benefits perform during major market corrections

Executive conclusion

This Goldman Sachs Japan strategy report studies yuutai, the shareholder-benefit programs common in Japan, and finds that companies offering such benefits tend to behave differently during market stress. The main conclusion is that yuutai stocks, especially those whose benefits are tied to the company's core business and those with minimum holding-period requirements, have historically shown better downside resilience during major TOPIX corrections. The report connects this effect to domestic retail investor loyalty, shareholder-base stability, NISA account growth, and the behavioral value of receiving tangible benefits. For investors, yuutai is not only a consumer perk; it may be a signal of sticky retail ownership and lower selling pressure in drawdowns.

What yuutai is

Yuutai refers to shareholder benefits offered by Japanese listed companies, often to domestic retail shareholders. Benefits can include the company's products, vouchers, discounts, meal coupons, travel benefits, retail gift certificates, or other perks. The benefits are often designed to connect shareholders emotionally to the company's products and services. Because many benefits are practical and familiar to households, yuutai can attract long-term retail holders who may be less sensitive to short-term market volatility than institutional investors.

Adoption data

About one-third of liquid listed Japanese companies, defined as those with more than USD5 million average daily trading value, have yuutai programs. The number has grown from about 170 companies to more than 240 between 2022 and 2026. Among companies with market capitalization above JPY100 billion, around 35-40% have such programs. This shows that yuutai is not limited to tiny illiquid companies; it is present across a meaningful part of the investable Japanese market.

Shareholder-base effects

Companies with yuutai programs tend to have nearly twice as many shareholders as comparable non-yuutai companies. The report's logic is that benefits broaden the shareholder base, especially among domestic individuals. A larger and more loyal retail base can reduce realized volatility and selling pressure during corrections. The exact volatility comparison in the report should be read carefully, but the qualitative conclusion is clear: yuutai companies are associated with a more stable shareholder structure and better downside behavior.

Correction performance

Goldman examines ten recent TOPIX corrections of more than 5% peak-to-trough. Across these drawdowns, yuutai companies outperformed by an average of nearly 3 percentage points per correction. This is the central empirical finding. The effect is not about yuutai companies always outperforming in all markets; it is specifically about market stress and downside resilience.

The structure of the benefit matters. During the February-March correction, when TOPIX fell about 11%, companies whose benefits were tied to their core business outperformed TOPIX by about 4.4 percentage points and outperformed other yuutai companies by about 1.9 percentage points. Core-business benefits may create stronger loyalty because shareholders understand and use the company's actual products. Programs with minimum holding-period requirements also tend to outperform because they discourage short-term trading and reward patient holders.

Factor and strategy evidence

A long yuutai-plus-dividend portfolio versus a short no-yuutai-no-dividend portfolio generated an average annual return of about 18% over the past five years. This is a strong result, though investors should not assume it is purely a yuutai effect; dividend policy, company quality, domestic ownership, and factor exposures may also contribute. Still, the finding suggests that shareholder-return policies aimed at households can matter in Japanese equity selection.

NISA and domestic retail backdrop

The report links yuutai to the growth of NISA accounts. NISA accounts exceeded 28 million by the end of 2025. Domestic stocks accounted for about 44% of NISA buying in January-March 2026. Japanese households still hold about 48.5% of assets in cash and deposits, indicating a large potential pool for further equity participation. Yuutai can be a bridge between cash-heavy households and equity ownership because it makes investing tangible.

Investment advice

For Japan equity investors, yuutai can be used as a defensive quality/ownership signal. Prefer companies with core-business-related benefits, minimum holding requirements, dividends, and broad domestic retail appeal. The strategy may be especially useful in corrections or volatile markets. It is not a replacement for fundamental analysis: valuation, earnings, governance, liquidity, and shareholder returns still matter. But in Japan, where household cash balances are large and corporate governance reform is ongoing, yuutai can help identify companies with a more stable shareholder base.

Risks

Risks include confusing correlation with causation, overpaying for low-growth defensive names, policy changes to benefit programs, illiquidity in smaller yuutai companies, and fading outperformance if too many investors crowd into the factor. Investors should monitor NISA flows, retail participation, dividend policy, and whether yuutai programs remain economically meaningful rather than symbolic.
