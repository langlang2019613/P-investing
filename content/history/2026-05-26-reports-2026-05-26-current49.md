## April FOMC Recap

English standalone summary

Source file: 1.US Daily_ April FOMC Recap_ A “Vigorous Discussion” About the Policy Guidance(1)(1).pdf
Duplicate note: Three exact duplicate files were selected; this report is summarized once.

Conclusion: Goldman reads the April FOMC meeting as modestly hawkish. The Fed left the fed funds target range and policy guidance unchanged, but the internal balance of the Committee shifted: three participants dissented against the statement’s implicit easing bias, while one dissented in favor of a cut. Chair Powell said the Committee had a “vigorous discussion” about guidance and that more participants could now support moving toward balanced guidance. Goldman’s key conclusion is that the Fed is closer to neutral language, and the risk to the September/December cut forecast is now tilted toward a longer pause.

Key data and arguments: The dissents came from Presidents Hammack, Kashkari, and Logan against the easing bias, plus Governor Miran in favor of a 25bp cut. Powell repeated that the FOMC can “wait here and see.” The pressure to move away from easing bias is tied mainly to inflation upside risks from the war and higher energy prices. Goldman also notes that some policymakers may hesitate to cut even after the war if inflation remains nearer 3% than 2%, even if the overshoot comes mostly from tariffs and energy pass-through.

Investment and market implications: Short rates moved higher, partly because the meeting was taken hawkishly and partly because oil kept rising on fears of a longer Strait of Hormuz disruption. Goldman remains skeptical of hikes, but thinks cuts require labor-market softening. This is bearish for aggressive rate-cut trades and supports a longer higher-for-longer rates stance unless payroll and survey data weaken meaningfully.

## Private Credit Cracks

English standalone summary

Source file: 7.Top of Mind_ Cracks in private credit(3).pdf
Duplicate note: Three exact duplicate files were selected; this report is summarized once.

Conclusion: Goldman’s Top of Mind issue asks whether private credit stress could become systemic. The answer from Howard Marks, Michael Arougheti, Bruce Richards, Amanda Lynam, and Goldman specialists is mostly “not systemic,” but with meaningful disagreement about the near-term default cycle. The stress is real: high-profile defaults, concerns over inflated valuations, retail redemption requests, and heavy software exposure have damaged confidence. But the structure of private credit, especially locked-up capital and limited fund leverage, reduces the risk of forced fire sales.

Systemic-risk argument: Arougheti and Lynam argue worries are concentrated in non-traded BDCs, which are only a modest part of private credit. Limited liquidity is a feature because it prevents a bank-run dynamic. Goldman also notes private credit funds have limited leverage, bank exposure is generally secured/protected, and even a sharp rise in default rates would only moderately affect new lending to the private sector. The comparison to the Global Financial Crisis is therefore viewed as exaggerated.

Software risk debate: Bruce Richards is the most bearish. He argues software loans are 3% of the high-yield bond market, 13% of broadly syndicated loans, but 23% of private credit, creating excessive concentration. He expects very high software-sector default rates in 2027-2029, peaking around 15%, with double-digit defaults in each year and loss rates of 70-100% on some software loans. Arougheti and Lynam are less worried because loans are senior secured and often backed by large cash-generative companies.

Investment advice: Private credit likely survives and keeps growing, but dispersion between managers should rise sharply. Institutional/opportunistic credit may benefit as retail-focused strategies slow. Favor high-quality alternative asset managers with disciplined underwriting and diversified credit platforms; avoid assuming private credit is risk-free simply because systemic risk appears limited.

## Energy Costs Inflation Outlook

English standalone summary

Source file: 8.US Economics Analyst_ Higher Energy Costs, International Spillovers, and the Inflation Outlook(1)(1).pdf
Duplicate note: Three exact duplicate files were selected; this report is summarized once.

Conclusion: Goldman raises its US inflation forecasts because higher energy costs and international spillovers from the Middle East shock are now larger than previously expected. The baseline assumes oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz normalize only by end-June, with about 0.5 mb/d of lasting Gulf production damage. Brent is expected to average $100/bbl in April and May before falling to $90 by 4Q26, with risks skewed upward.

Scenario data: In a benign case, flows recover by mid-June with no lasting damage and Brent falls to $80 by 4Q26. In an adverse case, normalization is delayed until end-July, Brent reaches $125 in May and $100 by Q4. In a severely adverse case with 2.5 mb/d lasting capacity loss, Brent reaches $145 in May and $120 by Q4.

Inflation impact: Goldman’s commodity pass-through framework now implies a 0.35 percentage point boost to core PCE and a 1.25 percentage point boost to headline PCE this year, capturing energy, food via fertilizer, and other Gulf-related non-energy exports. Foreign energy cost spillovers through import prices are expected to add about 0.1 percentage point to US core inflation in the baseline. The effect is modest because Asia and Europe export many finished consumer goods whose prices are less energy-sensitive.

Forecast changes: Goldman raises December 2026 core PCE to 2.6% year-on-year from 2.5%, and headline PCE to 3.4% from 3.1%. In adverse/severely adverse cases, core PCE rises to 2.7%/2.9%, and headline PCE to 3.7%/4.3%. Investment implication: inflation risk limits Fed easing, especially if supply-chain disruptions move beyond air freight into diesel, fertilizer, chemicals, and intermediate goods.

## JPY Funding

English standalone summary

Source file: 10.FX in Focus_ Verbal Intervention Not Enough to Challenge JPY Funding(1)(1).pdf
Duplicate note: Three exact duplicate files were selected; this report is summarized once.

Conclusion: Goldman argues that Japanese verbal intervention is not enough to undermine the yen’s role as a tactical funding currency. The trade-weighted yen is at its weakest level since the early 1990s and the Ministry of Finance has become more vocal about currency weakness, but the case for imminent direct FX intervention is weaker than in 2024 or even the January 2026 rate-check episode.

Intervention framework: Goldman says three usual prerequisites matter: a rapid move to very weak levels, dislocation from fundamentals, and forceful verbal intervention. Two of the first two conditions look less compelling now. On a real trade-weighted basis versus its 10-year rolling average, the yen is about 24% below average, still short of the 26-27% deviation before the 2024 interventions. The recent move has not been rapid, and volatility has been subdued. The condition would be more likely met if USD/JPY quickly rose to 163-164.

Model and positioning: USD/JPY has traded broadly in line with Goldman’s GSBEER model, unlike 2024 when it underperformed model-implied value by 4-6% before interventions. Positioning also does not show excessive speculative short yen. Futures positioning is not extremely short JPY, and options positioning suggests net long JPY versus USD, partly because investors already expect intervention risk. This makes immediate action less likely.

Investment implications: Goldman sees more depreciation pressure in an energy-led inflation shock if risk sentiment remains supported, making yen funding attractive tactically. However, recession risk would create asymmetric yen-strength risk. Direct intervention probability rises if USD/JPY breaks quickly toward 163-164 or if JGB/UST volatility spikes again.

## China Medtech Surgical Robots

English standalone summary

Source file: Goldman Sachs-CHINA MEDTECH GOING GLOBAL：Assessing the opportunity for surgical robots~global expansion beyond ISRG’s core markets-260421_加水印.pdf

Conclusion: Goldman sees laparoscopic surgical robots as one of the most attractive global expansion opportunities for Chinese medtech companies. The market has a proven razor-and-blade model, long-duration growth, and meaningful underpenetration outside the US. Chinese firms are unlikely to challenge Intuitive Surgical’s US leadership near term, but they can scale as value-oriented challengers in price-sensitive ex-US markets where ISRG’s ecosystem advantages are less entrenched.

Market size and data: Goldman expects the global endoscopic surgical robot TAM to grow from about $11bn in 2025 to $42bn by 2035, a 14% CAGR. Around 45% of the 2035 TAM should come from outside-US markets. ISRG’s global installed base reached 11,106 systems in 2025, versus cumulative orders of 220+ for MicroPort MedBot and 120+ for Edge Medical as of March 2026. In 2025, ISRG recorded 734 overseas system sales, while MedBot secured 100+ overseas orders and Edge Medical 70+, showing early catch-up momentum.

Why Chinese players can win: Chinese systems typically price equipment and consumables at a 30-40% discount to da Vinci, creating earlier hospital break-even and higher IRR. They also offer remote surgery, modular designs, Edge Medical’s 3-in-1 solution, and lower-cost versions for emerging markets. Goldman’s stress tests show Chinese vendors can maintain positive cash margins even with 75-85% price cuts.

Competitive view: ISRG’s moat is less about patents and more about scale, data, digital ecosystem, installed base, and procedure experience. Earlier patent waves have largely expired or are circumventable, but ISRG’s system integration remains powerful.

Investment advice: Goldman favors MicroPort MedBot (Buy) for global commercial infrastructure and Edge Medical (Buy) for multi-port plus single-port strategy and distributor-led overseas execution. It expects MedBot and Edge to capture about 5.4% and 4.1% of global TAM by 2035E.

## China Exports Geopolitical Disruption

English standalone summary

Source file: 地缘扰动下的出口三问.pdf

Conclusion: This Caixin/Huatai-style macro article asks how Middle East disruption affects China’s exports through three channels: external demand, China’s export share, and export prices. The framework is: exports = external demand volume × China share × export price. The conclusion is balanced: short-term external-demand impact may be mild, China’s share and export prices can offset part of the drag, but if the Strait of Hormuz reopening disappoints or leaves permanent supply scars, the negative impact could become much larger.

External demand: Historical oil shocks transmit through inflation first, then soft data/manufacturing PMI and consumer confidence after 1-3 months, then growth after 1-2 quarters, and employment after 3-4 quarters. This shock differs from the 1970s because energy structures, reserves, policy tools, and US/Europe direct dependence are more favorable. The article estimates every 10% oil-price rise drags global growth by about 0.15 percentage point. Scenario estimates: optimistic case with 2026 oil average $80/bbl drags global growth 0.2pp and China exports 0.5-1pp; neutral case with $100/bbl drags global growth 0.4-0.6pp and China exports 2-3pp; pessimistic case with blockade into 2H and oil at $150/bbl drags global growth 1.7pp and China exports close to 10pp.

Export share: China’s supply-chain resilience can lift export share and offset external demand weakness. During 2020-2021 China’s global export share rose from about 13% in 2019 to over 15.5%. The current episode is unlikely to repeat the pandemic magnitude. The article thinks a 0.3-0.5 percentage point short-term share gain is more reasonable. Key mechanisms are substitution effects, cost effects, and demand creation.

Prices and sectors: Export prices lag PPI; a roughly 3pp PPI uplift can support export value, but import-price pass-through can hurt trade surplus. Strong pricing-power areas include AI-chain exports: integrated circuits +64%, storage components +103%, diodes +62%, ADP equipment parts +178%, CPUs +38%, transformers +36%, wires/cables +27%. Resources/materials also benefit: urea export volume +2453% and price +79%, tungsten price +219%, rare earths +42%. Investment theme: AI, supply-chain competitiveness, materials, shipbuilding, and energy-transition demand are better lines through geopolitical volatility.
