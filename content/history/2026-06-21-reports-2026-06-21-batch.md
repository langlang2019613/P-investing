## US Daily June FOMC Recap

# US Daily: June FOMC Recap - Hikes Look More Likely but Are Still Not the Base Case

Source file: `1.US Daily_ June FOMC Recap_ Hikes Look More Likely but Are Still Not Our Base Case(1)(1).pdf`

## Core Conclusion

This Goldman Sachs note argues that the June 2026 FOMC meeting was meaningfully more hawkish than expected, but not enough to change the base case that the Fed keeps the policy rate unchanged through the rest of 2026. The Fed left the target range for the funds rate at 3.5%-3.75%, removed earlier forward guidance that had pointed toward cuts, and published projections that showed a larger-than-expected group of officials leaning toward a rate hike. The practical message is that the bar for cuts has risen, the probability of hikes has increased, and the next several inflation prints matter more than before.

The most important surprise was the distribution of the 2026 dots. Nine FOMC participants projected at least one hike this year, versus Goldman’s expectation that only three would do so. Because Chair Kevin Warsh did not submit projections, the dots effectively created a 9-9 split between officials who favor at least one hike and those who favor unchanged policy or a cut. That is not a clean signal that the Committee is about to hike, but it does mean inflation and energy-shock risks are being taken seriously by a much larger bloc of policymakers.

## Key Data and Policy Details

The policy rate was held at 3.5%-3.75% with no immediate move. The statement was shortened, and prior language that suggested future cuts were likely was removed. The Summary of Economic Projections also shifted in a hawkish direction. The median projection for core PCE inflation in 2027 was raised to 2.5%, compared with Goldman’s expectation of 2.3%. That matters because 2027 inflation is far enough out that a higher forecast cannot be explained only by a one-off energy shock. It suggests at least some officials worry that the recent energy shock, sticky inflation, and still-solid labor market could keep inflation above target for longer.

Market pricing reacted immediately. Goldman notes that end-2026 fed funds pricing rose by about 20bp, while end-2027 pricing rose by about 11bp after the projections and press conference. The market therefore interpreted the meeting as a shift away from cuts and toward a more balanced, or even slightly hawkish, reaction function.

Goldman still argues that a 2026 hike is not the central case. The reason is that many voters likely prefer to wait for clarity on geopolitical and energy developments, especially the US-Iran situation and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz. If energy flows normalize and the inflation impulse fades, the June projections could become stale quickly. Goldman also expects the May core PCE print to look firm at around 31bp, but notes that roughly 12bp of that increase comes from financial services, which may not carry the same policy weight as broad-based price pressure.

## Warsh Agenda and Institutional Changes

The note also highlights Chair Warsh’s operational priorities. He described five internal workstreams: Fed communications, the balance sheet, data sources, productivity and jobs in an era of transformation, and the inflation framework. The most immediate market relevance is around communications and the SEP. Warsh suggested that press conferences may not need to occur after every meeting, which could reduce the market’s ability to extract incremental policy guidance at each FOMC date. A less frequent or less formulaic communication schedule could raise meeting-by-meeting uncertainty.

## Investment Implications

The main investment conclusion is that front-end rates are vulnerable to upside surprises in inflation, but Goldman does not recommend treating hikes as the base case yet. The meeting reduces the probability of near-term cuts and makes duration exposure more sensitive to inflation data. The dollar and short-end Treasury yields should remain reactive to monthly inflation, wage, and energy-price data.

For equities, the implication is mixed. A hawkish Fed is a headwind for long-duration growth assets and companies dependent on cheap capital, but the reason for hawkishness matters. If the Fed is reacting to resilient growth and sticky inflation rather than recession risk, cyclicals may hold up better than in a classic tightening scare. The most important signposts are core PCE breadth, labor-market momentum, energy normalization, and whether the hawkish 2026 dots persist into later communications.

## Americas Technology IT Spending Survey

# Americas Technology: IT Spending Survey - AI Momentum Continues to Build While the Broader Outlook Softens

Source file: `10.Americas Technology_ IT Spending Survey_ AI momentum continues to build against a more muted broader outlook (3)(1).pdf`

## Core Conclusion

The survey shows a two-track enterprise technology market. Broad IT spending is still expanding, but momentum is weaker than it was in late 2024 and early 2025. At the same time, AI, cloud migration, security, servers, storage, and AI-related data infrastructure remain strong. Goldman’s conclusion is that AI is not yet consuming a large share of the average enterprise IT budget, but it is increasingly shaping vendor priorities, cloud architecture, security budgets, and capital allocation decisions. This supports a selective technology investment stance: favor AI infrastructure, hyperscale cloud exposure, security platforms, and data-management enablers; be more cautious on discretionary IT services, weaker networking categories, and software vendors whose budgets are being raided to fund AI.

## Broad IT Spending Outlook

The headline IT Spending Index fell to 65.0 from 68.0, while the IT Capital Spending Index fell to 59.0 from 65.5. Both remain above 50, meaning more respondents still expect increases than decreases, but both are below their 10-year averages of 73.5 and 67.1. For the second half of 2026, 53% of respondents expect overall IT spending to increase and 29% expect it to decline. For IT capex, 48% expect an increase, 29% expect a decrease, and 23% expect flat spending. This is not a collapse, but it is a more uneven environment with wider dispersion between categories.

## AI Budgeting and Funding

AI remains strategically important but is still early in budget penetration. Over the next 12 months, 88% of CIOs expect AI to represent less than 10% of the IT budget, with a weighted average around 5%. Over a three-year horizon, 42% expect generative AI to exceed 10% of the budget, lifting the weighted average to about 10%. Inference costs are also still modest for most enterprises: 89% expect inference to consume only 1%-5% of the IT budget.

Funding is a key constraint. Only about one-third of AI spending is expected to come from new budget. Roughly two-thirds must be reallocated from other categories. Sources of funding include cost efficiencies, application software, labor, cloud infrastructure, and data or analytics budgets. This is a major investment point: AI can be bullish for some vendors and bearish for others at the same time. Vendors tied directly to AI deployment can gain wallet share, while less strategic software or services categories may face pressure.

The survey also suggests enterprises are leaning toward buying AI capabilities rather than building everything internally. A majority, 56%, expect to build less and buy more, with 35% viewing this as structural and 21% saying it could change. This supports packaged software platforms, cloud AI services, and vendors that can embed AI into existing enterprise workflows.

## Cloud and Vendor Positioning

Cloud penetration continues to rise. Respondents estimate that 27% of workloads are currently in the cloud, rising to 47% over three years. Generative AI workloads in the cloud are expected to rise from 6% in one year to 15% in three years. Microsoft Azure and AWS remain dominant: Azure is estimated at 46% share today and 48% in three years, while AWS moves from 30% to 32%. Neocloud providers still have low enterprise penetration, generally under 5% compute share, but remain relevant for AI-native demand and GPU availability.

Strategic-vendor rankings favor Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Cisco, and Oracle, with Anthropic entering the top 10 on a three-year view. AI wallet-share scores are strongest for Anthropic, Microsoft, Google, and OpenAI. Snowflake, Salesforce, ServiceNow, and Databricks are in the middle tier, while Workday, Palantir, Oracle, Adobe, and SAP score weaker in this survey. The key idea is that enterprise AI value is concentrating around models, cloud platforms, data platforms, and workflow integration.

## Hardware, Services, and Security

Hardware looks better than broad IT. Server spending expectations improved sharply, with 60% expecting increases versus 37% previously. Storage also improved, with 75% expecting growth. PC spending expectations improved materially as well. Networking is weaker, with only 35% expecting increases, 31% expecting decreases, and 34% flat. Vendor momentum is mixed: Cisco and F5 show modest positive signals, while Arista, Dell EMC, HPE Aruba, and Juniper show weaker net readings in this survey.

IT services are under pressure. Only 28% expect discretionary services spending to increase, down from 40% previously, while 39% expect it to decline. A majority, 55%, plan to cut or delay external vendor spending. The main reason is budget constraint, followed by the need to fund other priorities and bring work in-house. AI savings are not automatically recycled back to services vendors; many enterprises plan to retain savings or redirect them to AI infrastructure.

Security remains a relative bright spot despite high valuation. Security stocks trade at a large premium to broader software, but 69% of respondents still expect generative AI to increase next-12-month security budgets. The weighted uplift is roughly 3.28%. Firewall refresh, SASE, and SOC modernization remain active categories. Microsoft, Palo Alto Networks, and Cloudflare screen well in security wallet share, while some legacy vendors score weaker.

## Investment Advice and Risks

The strongest investment takeaway is to stay constructive on AI infrastructure, leading cloud platforms, data-management vendors, and security platforms, while being selective in software and cautious on discretionary IT services. AI is a budget reallocator, not only a budget expander. The biggest risk to the bullish AI narrative is that enterprises remain slow to monetize AI or fund deployments mostly by cutting other technology categories. The best-positioned companies are those that either own the infrastructure layer, control enterprise distribution, or make AI deployments measurable and operationally useful.

## USA FOMC Leaves Rate Unchanged

# USA: FOMC Leaves Fed Funds Rate Unchanged; Dots Split Between No Move and a 2026 Hike

Source file: `2.USA_ FOMC Leaves Fed Funds Rate Unchanged; Median Dot Tied Between Unchanged Policy and One Hike in 2026; SEP Raises Core Inf...(1)(1).pdf`

## Core Conclusion

This shorter Goldman note is the immediate FOMC reaction piece. It confirms that the Fed left the funds rate unchanged at 3.5%-3.75% with no dissents, but the real message came from the projections. The June SEP moved in a clearly hawkish direction: inflation forecasts were revised higher, the unemployment forecast was slightly lower, and the 2026 dot distribution showed an even split between unchanged policy and at least one hike. The note’s practical conclusion is that the Fed is no longer guiding toward cuts and is increasingly prepared to defend price stability if inflation remains sticky.

## Policy Decision and Statement

The FOMC kept the target range for the federal funds rate unchanged. The statement continued to say inflation remains elevated and added emphasis on supply shocks, including energy. The Committee reaffirmed its commitment to returning inflation to target and maintained the balance-sheet approach based on an ample-reserves framework. No officials dissented from the decision, which means the disagreement is not about the June rate decision itself, but about the path from here.

The statement change is important because prior guidance had been more consistent with eventual easing. In this meeting, that easing bias was effectively removed. The Fed is signaling optionality: it can stay on hold if inflation improves, but it can hike if inflation remains too high or if energy and supply shocks spill over into broader prices.

## SEP and Dot Plot Details

The dot plot is the key data point. Nine participants projected at least one hike in 2026, eight projected unchanged policy, and one projected a cut. Chair Kevin Warsh did not submit projections. This creates a tied median around unchanged policy versus one hike, a much more hawkish setup than the market and Goldman expected before the meeting.

The inflation projections changed significantly. The 2026 headline PCE forecast was raised by 0.9 percentage points to 3.6%. The 2026 core PCE forecast was raised by 0.6 percentage points to 3.3%. For 2027, the core PCE forecast rose by 0.3 percentage points to 2.5%. That last revision is especially important because it points to inflation persistence beyond the immediate shock window. GDP was revised down slightly, by 0.2 percentage points to 2.2% for 2026, while the unemployment forecast was reduced by 0.1 percentage points to 4.3%. In plain English, the SEP says inflation is worse, growth is only modestly weaker, and the labor market is not weak enough to force the Fed to cut.

## How This Fits With the Broader Macro Picture

This note should be read together with the longer FOMC recap. The Fed is responding to a mix of sticky underlying inflation, energy-price risk, and uncertainty around geopolitical normalization. A US-Iran deal and Strait of Hormuz reopening could reduce energy inflation pressure, but policymakers appear unwilling to pre-commit to easing while the data remain uncomfortable.

The meeting also shows that the Fed’s reaction function is more symmetric than before. Earlier in the cycle, investors may have assumed that the next move was more likely down than up. After this meeting, a hike is a real scenario even if it is not the base case. That changes how investors should interpret strong data: firm inflation or solid labor-market prints now raise the risk of tightening, not merely a delay in easing.

## Investment Implications

The first-order market effect is negative for front-end duration. Two-year yields and short-rate futures are more vulnerable to upside inflation surprises. The second-order effect is higher dispersion across equities. Companies with strong pricing power, low leverage, and self-funded growth can handle a higher-for-longer environment better than companies dependent on cheap financing or aggressive multiple expansion.

For portfolio positioning, this report supports a more cautious stance on rate-sensitive long-duration assets until inflation data improve. It does not necessarily imply a recession trade, because the Fed is not cutting due to a weak economy. Instead, it implies a policy-volatility trade: markets will react strongly to core PCE, wage growth, energy prices, and FOMC communication. The biggest upside risk for bonds and growth stocks would be rapid energy normalization and softer core inflation. The biggest downside risk would be a series of firm inflation prints that turns the June hawkish dots into an actual hike.

## Oil Tracker Hormuz Flows

# Oil Tracker: 70% of Pre-War Hormuz Flows Might Become the New 100%

Source file: `3.Oil Tracker_ 70% of Pre-War Hormuz Flows Might Become the New 100%(1)(1).pdf`

## Core Conclusion

Goldman’s oil tracker argues that the crude market has moved from extreme supply-shock risk toward a normalization scenario, but normalization may not mean an immediate return to pre-war flow levels. Brent fell below $80 after the US-Iran interim deal, which is expected to lift the US blockade and reopen the Strait of Hormuz after signing. Goldman assumes Persian Gulf exports normalize by the end of July and crude production by October. Even so, the report’s title captures the key operational idea: roughly 70% of pre-war Hormuz flows may become the practical near-term definition of full normalization.

The market implication is that oil has downside risk if exports recover faster than expected, but upside risk remains if shipping, insurance, Iranian political strategy, or nuclear-negotiation uncertainty slows the return of barrels. The report is highly flow-driven: investors should watch actual tanker movements, Gulf of Oman dark flows, rerouting volumes, and China demand rather than relying only on diplomatic headlines.

## Hormuz Flow Data

Goldman estimates that visible Hormuz flows were only about 1.3mb/d over the prior seven days. Gulf of Oman flows were around 1.6mb/d and may include some dark or less-visible Hormuz crossings. Redirections through alternative export routes such as Yanbu, Fujairah, and Ceyhan were around 7.5mb/d. Based on these figures, the current hit to Persian Gulf oil flows was estimated at about 14.8mb/d on a four-day moving-average basis.

To normalize to a workable post-deal state, Goldman estimates that Hormuz flows need to rise by roughly 12.7mb/d to 13.0mb/d from current levels. That would still represent only around 70% of pre-war flows, which is why the report frames 70% as the possible new 100%. In other words, the market may not need every pre-war barrel to return before pricing a normalization scenario, but it does need clear evidence of materially higher flows.

Ship availability does not appear to be the main bottleneck. Goldman counts around 861mb to 860mb of empty tanker capacity within the Strait or within five days’ navigation. The more important constraints are behavioral and political: shipowners may wait for clearer transit guarantees, insurers may price risk cautiously, and Iran may use the 60-day nuclear negotiation window to manage the pace of export normalization.

## Iranian Barrels and Immediate Supply

The interim deal reportedly includes waivers for Iranian oil and petrochemical exports as well as related financial and transportation services. That could unlock more than 50mb of Iranian oil already on the water for immediate delivery. If released quickly, these barrels could pressure prompt crude prices and spreads. But if the release is delayed by compliance, banking, or shipping frictions, the bearish price impact may be slower.

## Demand Destruction and Inventory Signals

The IEA’s June Oil Market Report is an important comparison point. The IEA estimated a Q2 deficit of 3.1mb/d, smaller than Goldman’s 5.0mb/d estimate, because the IEA assumes larger demand destruction: 5.5mb/d versus Goldman’s 4.9mb/d. May demand destruction was estimated at 6.1mb/d. Product-level weakness was broad: LPG and ethane demand was down about 1.7mb/d, naphtha down 1.1mb/d, diesel and gasoil down 1.2mb/d, gasoline down 0.8mb/d, and jet fuel down 0.5mb/d.

China is central to the demand story. Goldman estimates China represents roughly 40% of global oil demand destruction, or around 2mb/d. Chinese crude imports fell by about 4.2mb/d year over year. Goldman also estimates that China crude stocks drew down by about 1.6mb/d in May, while visible inventories only showed a 0.3mb/d draw, implying about 1.3mb/d of invisible destocking or lower crude demand and refinery runs. China retail oil product sales volumes fell 23.3% year over year in May. In Europe, road fuels were down 3.5%.

Global visible stock draws remain large. Since March 1, global visible stocks have drawn at around 3.4mb/d, including June at 2.2mb/d, May at 5.5mb/d, and April at 2.0mb/d. Global crude and condensate imports are down about 5.8mb/d from the 2025 average, while product imports are down about 4.5mb/d.

## Investment Implications

The report supports a balanced but tactically cautious view on crude. If flows normalize quickly and Iranian barrels hit the market, crude prices can fall further. If normalization stalls, the market could rebuild a risk premium quickly because the absolute flow numbers remain very large. Energy equities, refiners, shippers, and commodity currencies will react differently depending on whether price moves are driven by supply recovery or demand weakness. The key dashboard should include Brent time spreads, actual Hormuz crossings, Gulf of Oman tracking, Chinese crude imports, Chinese product sales, and visible inventory draws.

## Macro Research Top of Mind

# What Is Top of Mind in Macro Research: US-Iran Deal, Hawkish Fed, Central Bank Holds, and the Capex Supercycle

Source file: `4.What's Top of Mind in Macro Research_ US-Iran deal, hawkish Fed surprise and continued central bank holds, capex supercycle(1)(1).pdf`

## Core Conclusion

This cross-asset Goldman macro note ties together four dominant market themes: a US-Iran deal that reduces energy tail risk, a hawkish Fed surprise, continued caution from major central banks, and a structural capex supercycle driven by AI, energy security, defense, and supply-chain resilience. The conclusion is not a simple risk-on or risk-off call. Energy normalization supports growth and helps inflation, but central banks remain cautious, and the capex boom shifts market leadership toward capital beneficiaries rather than broad multiple expansion.

## US-Iran Deal and Energy Price Forecasts

Goldman’s energy team assumes Persian Gulf oil exports normalize by the end of July, earlier than the prior assumption of end-August. Because of that, the 4Q26 Brent forecast was cut to $80/bbl from $90/bbl, and the 2027 average forecast was cut to $75/bbl from $80/bbl. Risks remain two-sided, but the report characterizes the net risk as still to the upside because shipping and political normalization may be slower than headline diplomacy implies.

For European gas, Goldman expects LNG flows to normalize by the end of July. That should be sufficient for Europe to refill storage without requiring TTF prices to rise enough to pull cargoes away from Asia. TTF forecasts are around €42/MWh for 3Q26, €40/MWh for 4Q26, and €30/MWh for the 2027 average. The risk skew is still to the upside if LNG normalization disappoints. Lower energy prices and incoming data caused Goldman to lower the expected Euro area peak for core and headline inflation to 2.6% and 3.2%, from 2.7% and 3.4%.

## Market Reaction to the Deal

The deal produced a relief rally. Procyclical and energy-importer currencies outperformed, while energy risk premia declined. However, Goldman cautions that further rates relief may be limited because markets had already priced some energy improvement and because underlying inflation remains sticky. In FX, a larger reversal would require actual evidence of flow recovery, not only diplomatic announcements.

## Fed and Central Banks

The FOMC left rates unchanged, but the meeting was more hawkish than expected. Nine participants projected a 2026 hike, and the median 2027 core PCE projection rose to 2.5%. Goldman still expects the Fed to remain unchanged this year, but the meeting clearly raises the risk that strong inflation data can trigger tighter policy.

Outside the US, central banks also remain cautious. Goldman expected the Riksbank, Bank of England, Swiss National Bank, and Norges Bank to hold near term. The BoE is expected to remain unchanged for the rest of the year. The SNB is described as comfortably on hold. Norges Bank and the Riksbank are more likely to hike later, around September and December respectively. The Bank of Japan has already hiked, but Goldman expects the next hike in January 2027, with uncertainty. The BOJ’s gradual pace supports JGB underperformance and a weak yen bias.

## Capex Supercycle

The structural equity theme is the capex supercycle. Private investment is being led by AI, while governments are spending more on energy security, defense, infrastructure, and supply-chain resilience. Goldman highlights CAPEX Beneficiaries and Capital Intensive baskets as potential winners. The report also notes that AI capex could weigh on mega-cap tech return on equity in the near term because depreciation and funding needs rise before productivity benefits fully arrive. Over the long term, productivity gains may benefit a broader set of companies beyond the direct AI infrastructure winners.

## Credit and Consumer Stress

The report flags stress in credit cards and autos. Ninety-plus-day delinquency rates for credit cards and auto loans are near Global Financial Crisis peaks, though transition rates into delinquency are still below crisis peaks. Stress is concentrated among subprime borrowers, especially in subprime auto ABS, where Goldman remains underweight.

## Market Data and Forecast Anchors

Goldman’s forecast table shows global GDP growth at 2.2% in 2026 and 2.4% in 2027. US growth is forecast at 2.0% and 2.1%, China at 4.6% and 4.7%, and the Euro area at 0.2% and 0.3%. The S&P 500 target for 2026 is 8000, with EPS of 340 and a forward P/E around 22.9x. Brent is forecast at $80 in 4Q26, and TTF at €40/MWh in 4Q26.

## Investment Advice

The best reading of the note is to combine tactical energy relief with structural capex leadership. Lower oil and gas reduce inflation tail risk, but investors should not assume central banks will quickly ease. Equity exposure should favor companies with direct leverage to AI infrastructure, electrification, power, defense, industrial automation, and supply-chain rebuilding. Credit risk should be managed carefully in subprime-sensitive consumer areas. The main risks are a renewed energy disruption, another inflation surprise that forces hikes, and overpaying for AI beneficiaries whose near-term ROE is diluted by heavy capex.

## Post Modern Cycle Capex Boom

# Global Strategy Paper: The Post Modern Cycle - Navigating the Capex Boom

Source file: `5.Global Strategy Paper_ The Post Modern Cycle – Navigating the Capex Boom(3)(1).pdf`

## Core Conclusion

Goldman argues that the market has moved into a “Post Modern” cycle, a regime very different from the disinflationary, globalization-heavy, falling-rate world that supported asset prices for decades. The new cycle is defined by higher macro volatility, higher real rates, larger government debt, greater state intervention, tariffs, regionalization, energy-security spending, defense spending, and an AI-led capex boom. The central investment conclusion is that returns are likely to depend more on earnings growth and real investment beneficiaries than on broad valuation expansion.

In this regime, the winners are companies that benefit from higher capital spending: semiconductors, power and electrification, defense, industrials, commodity producers, infrastructure suppliers, and selected technology hardware. Capital-light growth and long-duration software can still win selectively, but the automatic dominance of capital-light growth stocks is less reliable than in the zero-rate era.

## Regime Shift: From Modern to Zero-Rate to Post Modern

The Modern cycle from the early 1980s to 2007 benefited from disinflation, deregulation, lower taxes, globalization, falling tariffs, low defense burdens, lower labor and energy costs, and expanding margins. The zero-rate period after the Global Financial Crisis produced weaker GDP recovery but very strong financial-asset returns because QE and low discount rates supported valuation expansion. That environment rewarded US equities, mega-cap technology, growth stocks, and capital-light business models.

The Post Modern cycle is different. Government debt is much higher: US public debt to GDP rose from around 55% to 124%, UK debt from 37% to 95%, Euro area debt from 69% to 95%, and China from 22% to 102%. Long rates have also changed. German and Japanese 30-year yields are near 4%, compared with near-zero levels not long ago. US tariffs are described as the highest since the 1930s. These conditions make the cost of capital higher and make governments more active participants in markets.

## Why Capex Is the Dominant Theme

The capex boom has multiple engines. AI requires data centers, GPUs, power, cooling, networking, and energy infrastructure. Energy security requires generation, transmission, storage, LNG, and grid resilience. Defense spending is rising as geopolitics becomes more fragmented. Supply-chain resilience requires regional manufacturing capacity, logistics, and industrial automation. These are physical investment cycles, not only software cycles.

Goldman notes that in Q1 2026, S&P 500 capex rose 38% year over year, while buybacks grew only 1%. The top five hyperscalers are expected to spend around $755bn in capex in 2026, up roughly 80% year over year, and about $920bn in 2027. This is extraordinary scale. It supports semiconductor and infrastructure suppliers, but it also raises questions about hyperscaler ROE because depreciation and financing needs rise before the full revenue benefit is proven.

## Market Rotation and Sector Leadership

Since 2025, leadership has broadened away from the narrow zero-rate winners. Gold, emerging markets, Japan’s Topix, value stocks, and real assets have performed well. Technology remains important, but leadership inside technology has shifted toward hardware and semiconductors rather than software. Goldman notes that software’s valuation premium compressed meaningfully, while hardware and software valuation premiums converged. This reflects investor concern that AI capex may benefit chips and infrastructure first, while some software categories face disruption or budget reallocation.

Goldman’s CAPEX Beneficiaries universe is diversified but tilted to real economy sectors: around 30% industrials, 20% commodity producers, 15% technology, 10% utilities, with the rest in chemicals, construction, telecom, and real estate. Consumer, healthcare, and financial exposure is limited. The targeted baskets include AI, Defense Spending, Power and Electrification, and HALO-related themes.

## Earnings and Alpha

The report expects double-digit EPS growth for capex beneficiaries, with consensus earnings up around 25% year over year. A key observation is that performance in capex beneficiary baskets tends to lead the capex cycle by several quarters. In other words, the market prices the cycle before capex appears fully in realized earnings. This helps explain why many capex-exposed baskets have already performed strongly, but Goldman still views the structural backdrop as supportive.

The investment regime should also produce more dispersion and more alpha opportunity. When returns are driven by cheap capital and multiple expansion, broad factor exposure can work. When returns are driven by execution, capital discipline, supply-chain positioning, and government priorities, stock selection matters more.

## Investment Advice and Risks

The report supports overweight exposure to companies that sell into AI infrastructure, power and electrification, defense, industrial automation, energy security, and supply-chain resilience. It also supports more exposure to real assets and regions or sectors that benefit from capex rather than only from financial engineering. Investors should be careful with companies whose valuations assume zero-rate-era margins, low capex intensity, or easy funding.

The risks are valuation crowding, capex overbuild, policy reversal, delayed AI monetization, and margin pressure from depreciation. The best companies in this cycle will be those that convert high capex demand into durable earnings without destroying returns on capital. The report’s major message is not simply “buy capex”; it is “buy the beneficiaries of a structurally higher investment cycle, but distinguish suppliers with pricing power from spenders whose returns may dilute.”

## Spending Headwinds on Both Sides of the Pond

# Global Economics Comment: Spending Headwinds Still to Come on Both Sides of the Pond

Source file: `6.Global Economics Comment_ Spending Headwinds Still to Come on Both Sides of the Pond(1)(1).pdf`

## Core Conclusion

Goldman warns that lower energy tail risk after the US-Iran deal does not mean the consumer-spending drag has already passed. Energy shocks hit household cash flow with lags, and those lags are especially important in Europe because retail energy contracts, regulation, and winter demand delay the full effect. In the US, the temporary boost from tax refunds is fading, so real household cash flow may stagnate or decline in the second half of 2026. The conclusion is that investors should not be complacent about consumer spending even if spot energy prices have improved.

## Why the Drag Has Not Fully Arrived

The report highlights three reasons the spending headwind is still coming. First, wholesale gas and electricity prices do not pass through immediately to household bills. Many consumers face fixed contracts, regulated tariffs, or delayed pricing mechanisms. Second, winter seasonality amplifies the cash-flow hit because households consume more energy in colder months. Third, the US consumer received a spring boost from tax refunds, but that support is now behind us.

Goldman’s energy team lowered its Brent forecast after the US-Iran peace deal. The 4Q26 Brent forecast was cut to $80/bbl from $90. Gas forecasts were also lower near term, although year-end forecasts were broadly unchanged. Even with this improvement, the already-realized price shock still works through household budgets slowly.

## Europe and the Seasonal Energy Effect

Europe faces the clearest lagged impact because energy contracts and regulation slow pass-through. Countries also need to rebuild gas inventories for winter. Seasonal demand matters: Goldman estimates that 2027Q1 energy costs could reduce real incomes by another 14bp in Europe. That represents more than 20% of the total combined impulse from the energy shock. The idea is not that energy alone causes a severe downturn, but that it can be enough to soften discretionary spending at a time when growth is already weak.

The peak spending drag is estimated at about 0.4% for the Euro area and 0.3% for the UK. Goldman believes only about half of the peak drag has been realized by the end of Q2 in the Euro area, UK, and Canada. That leaves a meaningful additional drag for H2 2026 and early 2027.

## United States: Tax Refund Boost Reverses

In the US, the energy shock interacts with fiscal timing. Larger tax refunds boosted real household cash flow by about 0.8% in Q1 and 1.2% in Q2. That support fades in the second half. When combined with energy costs, Goldman estimates a swing from a +0.7% boost to real cash flow in Q2 to a -0.6% drag in Q3. This is a material change in the direction of household purchasing power.

The report uses historical relationships to translate real-income shocks into spending. A 1% decline in real income from an energy shock has historically reduced consumer spending by about 0.6% after two quarters. That lag explains why spending has not yet fully reacted, especially in the US.

## Cross-Country Spending Drag

Goldman estimates the peak spending drag at 0.5% in Canada, 0.4% in the Euro area, and 0.3% in both the US and UK. These numbers are not catastrophic, but they are large enough to matter for retailers, autos, travel, restaurants, durable goods, and lower-income consumer exposure. Consumer surveys already show fewer planned large purchases over the next 6-12 months, consistent with a delayed real-income squeeze.

## Investment Advice

The report argues for caution on consumer discretionary and durable-goods exposure, especially where demand depends on lower-income households or financing conditions. It also supports being selective within Europe, where winter energy bills can create a more visible drag. In the US, the near-term risk is that investors extrapolate resilient spring spending without recognizing that tax refunds pulled forward cash-flow strength.

For macro positioning, the note suggests that lower oil prices are helpful but not enough to guarantee stronger consumption. Rates markets may price energy relief, but central banks may still be cautious because inflation remains sticky. Equity investors should watch real income, retail sales breadth, credit-card delinquencies, auto delinquencies, and energy-bill pass-through. The best-positioned consumer companies are those with affluent customers, pricing power, low financing sensitivity, and limited exposure to big-ticket discretionary purchases.

## Chinese Household Balance Sheet

# Asia Economics Analyst: Chinese Household Balance Sheet - Structural Shift From Property to Financial Assets

Source file: `8.Asia Economics Analyst_ Chinese Household Balance Sheet_ Structural Shift from Property to Financial Assets Amid Ongoing Dele...(1).pdf`

## Core Conclusion

Goldman argues that China’s household balance sheet is undergoing a long structural transition away from property dominance and toward financial assets. This shift is gradual, uneven, and concentrated among more affluent households, but it has major macro and market implications. Property remains the largest store of household wealth, so the housing downturn still weighs heavily on confidence and consumption. However, lower deposit rates, policy support for capital markets, and the stabilization of property prices are slowly encouraging households to diversify into wealth-management products, insurance, funds, and equities.

## Household Asset Trends

Goldman built a quarterly tracker because official PBOC and CNBS household balance-sheet data are delayed. According to this tracker, Chinese household assets peaked in early 2023, declined for roughly six quarters through 2023 and 2024, and then stabilized recently around RMB730tn. The stabilization reflects two offsetting forces: property values remain under pressure, but financial assets have recovered since the September 2024 policy pivot and households continue to accumulate cash and deposits.

The composition shift is striking. In mid-2021, property represented about 67% of household assets, cash and deposits 16%, and other financial assets 15%. By 2026Q1, property had fallen to about 52%, cash and deposits had risen to 25%, and other financial assets had risen to 20%. Directly held equities within other financial assets increased from around 5% to 6%.

Longer-term CNBS data show household net worth to GDP rose from around 200% in 1997 to roughly 400% in 2022. Asset growth was extremely fast in earlier decades, annualizing around 25% from 1992 to 2002 and around 20% from 2002 to 2012, but leverage also rose quickly as liabilities grew faster than assets.

## Property Wealth Effect

Housing still dominates household wealth. Goldman notes that property prices are down around 30% from the peak. Because housing is roughly 60% of household assets while equities are less than 10%, the property wealth effect is much larger than the stock-market wealth effect. More than 90% of households own property, while only about 25% of Chinese adults participate in the equity market. This explains why rising equity markets alone cannot fully repair household confidence if property remains weak.

Goldman sees early signs of property stabilization. New and existing home sales have stabilized, 70-city price declines have eased, and Tier-1 new and existing home prices have risen. Goldman expects local prices in Tier-1 and Tier-2 cities to stabilize over the next one to two years, with Shenzhen and Shanghai leading. If this stabilization holds, it could help confidence, spending, and capital markets. A key risk is a new shock such as labor-market weakness or AI-related job displacement.

## Deleveraging and Household Liabilities

Household leverage has drifted lower since mid-2024, mainly because mortgage balances have declined. Short-term consumer loans also remain soft. Mortgage rate cuts and subsidies have only limited effect because confidence and labor-market expectations remain weak. Household debt to GDP peaked around 61% in early 2024 and was about 59% by Q3 2025. That is below the US at around 70% and Japan around 62%, but above the emerging-market average of about 45%.

Debt relative to disposable income is more concerning at around 140%, because Chinese household disposable income is only around 45% of GDP, compared with about 75% in the US. This means households can look moderately leveraged relative to GDP but still feel highly constrained relative to actual income.

## From Bricks to Beta

The report’s central market theme is a gradual move from “bricks” to “beta.” Falling deposit rates are an important catalyst. The three-year deposit rate has dropped to about 1.25%, from 1.95% in early 2024 and 2.60% in early 2023. The spread between three-year and one-year deposits has narrowed to about 30bp from 50bp-100bp. Lower returns on deposits make wealth-management products, insurance, mutual funds, and equities more attractive.

Goldman expects the equity share of household assets to rise gradually. Direct equity holdings could rise from about 6% today to around 11% by 2035 if they grow at a 10% annual rate. Property’s share could fall from 52% to 42% by 2035. Insurance could rise from 6% to 10%, implying around RMB6tn per year from valuation gains and net inflows.

## Investment Implications

The report is structurally positive for Chinese equities, insurance, wealth management, mutual funds, and capital-market intermediaries, but it does not predict an immediate household rotation into stocks. The shift is slow because precautionary savings remain high and property still dominates household psychology. The best investment setup is property stabilization plus lower deposit rates plus improving equity returns. The main risks are renewed housing weakness, weak employment, policy disappointment, and a capital-market rally that fails to broaden beyond affluent households.

## China Lujiazui Forum Monetary Policy

# China: Lujiazui Forum - Price-Based Monetary Policy and Rules-Based Capital Account Opening

Source file: `9.China_ Lujiazui Forum_ Price-based monetary policy and rules-based capital account opening(1).pdf`

## Core Conclusion

Goldman interprets the 2026 Lujiazui Forum as a clear signal that China is moving toward a more price-based monetary policy framework and a more rules-based approach to capital-account opening. The PBOC is reducing reliance on aggregate credit targets and moving closer to a developed-market-style interest-rate corridor. At the same time, SAFE and other regulators are not reversing capital-account opening; they are trying to channel persistent outbound demand into more compliant, transparent, and monitorable routes.

The investment conclusion is that slower loan growth should not automatically be read as monetary tightening. It partly reflects structural deleveraging in property and local-government financing vehicles, plus a shift from loan-heavy old-economy growth to direct financing for new-economy sectors. For markets, front-end rates may become less volatile, 10-year CGB yields may remain range-bound near 1.7%-1.8%, and capital-account channels such as QDII, Bond Connect, RMB futures, and offshore RMB infrastructure become more important.

## PBOC Corridor Reform

The PBOC is refining its short-term interest-rate corridor around the 7-day reverse repo rate, currently 1.4%. The temporary overnight repo and reverse repo corridor was adjusted from an asymmetric band of roughly 7-day reverse repo minus 20bp and plus 50bp, or 1.2%-1.9%, to a more symmetric plus/minus 25bp corridor, or 1.15%-1.65%. The PBOC will activate operations if DR001 persistently moves outside the corridor.

This means the operational focus shifts from DR007 toward DR001, with overnight funding rates anchored around the 7-day policy rate. The corridor should reduce funding volatility and cap stress during periods such as quarter-end. It also creates a more visible floor for front-end rates, making a return to the very low April-May funding levels less likely unless the PBOC cuts policy rates.

The PBOC also announced or discussed tools including a FIMA RMB Repo facility for foreign official institutions and a macroprudential liquidity-support tool for non-bank financial institutions under stress. These tools point to a more complete market-stability framework.

## Slower Credit Growth Is Structural

Goldman emphasizes that slower loan growth is not necessarily a sign of tightening. In 2025, loans accounted for 45% of incremental total social financing, while bonds and equities accounted for 47%, exceeding loans for the first time. New loans to property and infrastructure fell from more than 60% of new loans to around 10%. Meanwhile, loans to the “five major areas of finance” - technology, green finance, inclusive finance, elderly care, and the digital economy - rose to more than 70%.

This reflects the changing structure of China’s economy. Property and LGFV deleveraging reduce loan demand. New-economy companies are often asset-light, R&D-intensive, and better suited to equity, bonds, venture capital, private equity, or specialized financing. Goldman lowered its 2026 TSF growth forecast to 7.6% year over year from 8.0%, and also lowered outer-year expectations.

## Capital Account: More Opening, More Monitoring

The capital-account message is not “close the gate.” It is “open through rules.” Regulators want to facilitate compliant outbound direct investment, overseas bond and loan financing, and new QDII quotas. At the same time, they want better monitoring and fewer untraceable flows.

Resident demand for foreign assets remains strong. In 2025, Goldman estimates resident FX asset purchases created about $762bn of outflows. Portfolio outflows were about $361bn, or 47% of the total, with equities representing around 60% of that. Other investment outflows were about $283bn, or 37%, including cash, deposits, and loans. Outbound direct investment was about $157bn. These figures explain why regulators are focused on building controlled channels rather than trying to suppress demand completely.

## RMB Market Infrastructure

The forum also pointed to deeper RMB market infrastructure: five-year RMB Chinese government bond futures in Hong Kong, QFII access to onshore CGB futures, RMB FX futures pilots, the FIMA RMB repo facility, and offshore RMB initiatives in Shanghai. These measures can support RMB internationalization and provide more hedging tools for foreign investors.

## Investment Advice

For rates investors, the corridor reform suggests less extreme front-end volatility and a stronger policy-rate anchor. Recent repo-rate increases should be read as liquidity normalization rather than a tightening cycle. Weak April-May activity data argue against sustained tightening. Goldman expects 10-year CGB yields to remain range-bound around 1.7%-1.8%, with low rates and medium-term room for lower yields if growth disappoints.

For equities and financials, the broader implication is positive for direct financing, capital-market infrastructure, brokers, exchanges, derivatives, offshore RMB products, and sectors aligned with policy priorities. The main risks are excessive outflow pressure, renewed RMB depreciation, policy miscommunication, or a funding squeeze if corridor operations are not used flexibly.

## 上海样本 算电如何协同

# 最新封面报道之二：上海样本 - 算电如何协同？

来源文件：`最新封面报道之二上海样本算电如何协同.pdf`

## 核心结论

这篇报道的主线是：AI 算力需求正在把“电力系统”从幕后基础设施推到产业竞争的中心。上海临港的案例显示，未来先进算力中心的竞争力不只取决于芯片、机房和网络，还取决于绿电获取能力、电网接入能力、低时延调度能力以及政策结算机制。文章用两个代表性样本解释“算电协同”：一是海兰云海底数据中心与海上风电直连，二是中国电信从上海向西部绿色算力中心调度负载的“东数西算”实验。

总体看，这不是单纯的数据中心故事，而是 AI 时代电力、算力、绿电、通信网络和区域产业政策重新组合的故事。投资上，受益方向包括数据中心、电力设备、海上风电、输配电、储能、虚拟电厂、绿电交易、液冷/海水冷却、算力调度平台和云/电信运营商。但报道也清楚指出，成本、维护、政策结算、规模化、异构芯片迁移和可靠性仍是关键约束。

## 上海临港：算力需求推高电力需求

临港新片区成立于 2019 年 8 月，面积 873 平方公里，海岸线 56.6 公里，重点发展集成电路、智能新能源汽车、民用航空、生物医药和高端装备等产业。随着 AI 和先进制造集聚，电力需求快速增长。临港电网售电量从 2020 年的 29.14 亿千瓦时增至 2025 年的 81.09 亿千瓦时，年复合增速约 22.7%。2026 年 1-5 月平均增速为 29.77%，显著高于上海全市 6.32% 的增速。

用电结构也在变化。临港前 20 大用电企业中有 5 家是数据中心，增长最快的前三名全部是数据中心：联通数据中心用电增长 245%，中国移动增长 232%，中国电信增长 91%。这说明 AI 和数据中心已经成为局部电网负荷增长的核心来源。为匹配负荷增长，2025 年 5 月至 2026 年 6 月，临港新增 4 座 220kV 变电站，数量接近翻倍。

临港到 2025 年底的智能算力规模达到 40 EFLOPS，接近上海全市一半，预计 2026 年底再翻倍。商汤临港数据中心也显示出相似趋势：机柜需求旺盛，设备和机柜基本满载，调优频率从过去较低变成每 1-2 周调整一次。单机柜功率从 2022 年的 8-10kW，上升到 2023-2024 年的 20-25kW，现在最高可达 110kW，超过十倍增长。

## 海兰云：海上风电加海底数据中心

报道最有代表性的案例是临港附近的海兰云海底数据中心。项目距离海岸约 10 公里，附近有 50 多台海上风机。数据中心由海上平台和海底密封舱组成，舱体位于水下约 10 米，重量约 1950 吨，包含 192 个机柜。项目一期在 2026 年 2 月商业运行，二期计划在 2026 年内推进。

该模式的优势是接近上海本地算力需求、利用海上风电、通过海水自然冷却降低能耗，并保持毫秒级低时延。项目宣称绿电比例 95%，PUE 约 1.15，且不消耗淡水。这相当于把“东数西算”的一部分逻辑改成“东数海算”：不把全部算力搬到西部，而是在东部需求中心附近寻找海上空间和绿电资源。

海底数据中心并不是全新概念。微软曾在 2015 年尝试水下数据中心，但因成本和商业化问题没有继续扩大。国内此前在海南陵水也有试点，但早期完全沉入海底的模式维护成本较高，维修时可能需要吊起整个舱体，单次打捞成本可达 500 万至 1000 万元，且需要大量冗余。临港项目的改进是增加海上平台和更便利的维护结构，同时结合海上风电直供概念。

## 绿电结算是关键变量

报道指出，项目在物理上接近“风电直连数据中心”，但商业结算仍受旧模式约束。海上风电目前卖给电网，数据中心再从电网买电。数据中心购电价约 0.75 元/千瓦时，而申能海上风电上网电价约 0.35 元/千瓦时。如果未来能够适用绿电直连或专线结算政策，免除部分输配电费用，只缴纳政府性基金，数据中心电力成本可能显著下降。

这也是项目能否大规模复制的关键。报道认为，海底数据中心装机容量适合控制在风电场容量的约 10% 左右，以维持较高绿电比例。申能该风场容量为 212.8MW，海兰云两期合计约 24MW，接近这一比例。二期单位 MW 成本预计较一期降低约 50%，说明规模化后经济性可能改善。

## 东数西算和跨区域调度

另一条路径是把部分负载从上海调度到西部绿色算力中心。2026 年 5 月 28 日，中国电信完成从上海临港智算谷到新疆克拉玛依绿色算力中心、超过 4000 公里的调度测试，规模为 24 张算力卡。测试目标是在华东电网高峰时，将可迁移任务调往西部，使本地负载下降超过 75%。

此前也有类似试验：2025 年 7 月上海到湖北十堰，本地负载下降约 75%；2025 年 12 月上海到福州，百卡级集群迁移，本地负载下降约 80%。这些试验还涉及异构算力迁移，例如通过“息壤一体化智算平台”在昇腾和英伟达之间做任务适配。但报道强调，这仍处在试验和示范阶段，尚未形成大规模商业化。

## 投资启示和风险

这篇报道最重要的投资启示是：算力产业链未来会越来越像“能源加制造加通信”的综合基础设施，而不只是云计算或芯片。值得跟踪的方向包括海上风电运营商、电网设备、变电站、特高压、液冷和海水冷却、数据中心工程、绿色电力交易、电信运营商、算力调度平台、储能和需求响应。

主要风险也很明确：海底数据中心维护和冗余成本高；绿电结算政策尚未完全打通；海上风电出力波动需要备电和调度；跨区域算力迁移受网络、时延、数据安全、异构芯片和任务类型限制；高密度机柜对供电、散热和可靠性要求极高。结论是，算电协同方向确定，但真正大规模商业化还需要电价机制、工程成本和调度技术三方面同时成熟。

## 影响 IPO 后表现的关键变量

报告从历史样本中总结了几类重要变量。

第一是发行和上市结构。大市值、流动性较好、行业稀缺性较强的新股更容易获得机构关注。与 A 股、H 股、两地上市结构相关的估值差和流动性差也会影响短期表现。对于已经有 A 股可比或回归港股的公司，投资者会关注港股定价相对 A 股是否有折价，以及折价是否足以吸引南向资金和全球资金。

第二是散户认购热度。散户超额认购往往对短期表现有重要影响。高认购倍数反映市场情绪和资金追逐，但也可能推高上市首日估值，因此需要结合估值和流通盘判断。极端热门项目短期可能强势，但如果基本面不能支撑，中期也容易回落。

第三是基石投资者比例。报告认为，约 30%-50% 的基石投资者比例通常较为健康，可以向市场传递质量信号，也有助于稳定发行。但如果基石比例过高，上市后实际流通盘太小，流动性不足，股价可能更容易被短期资金放大波动。

第四是行业属性和成长性。成长行业、新经济、医疗健康、科技和其他稀缺赛道更容易获得估值溢价。估值本身不是唯一决定因素，市场更重视盈利增长路径、行业空间、商业模式壁垒和是否具备稀缺性。

## 板块外溢效应

报告指出，IPO 活跃度会对相关板块产生外溢影响。香港市场中 IPO 较多的板块，例如医疗健康和信息技术，在相关新股上市后一周内往往跑赢大盘。大型海外 IPO 也可能带动中国同类公司估值重估。这个逻辑适合用于寻找二级市场机会：如果某个细分行业有高质量 IPO 成功上市并获得较好定价，已上市可比公司可能也会受益。

## 解禁压力和供给风险

报告同时强调，香港 IPO 市场不是只有机会，也有供给压力。未来 12 个月可能有约 2740 亿美元或等值规模的限售股份解禁压力。历史上，解禁后 3-6 个月的平均收益往往较弱，可能下跌 4%-7%，同时个股分化加大。短期表现取决于解禁股数相对自由流通盘的比例，以及解禁前股价已经上涨多少。中期表现则取决于公司质量、自由流通比例、市场流动性和核心股东是否减持。

这部分对投资非常关键。IPO 后涨幅很大的股票，如果解禁比例高、流通盘小、估值已经透支，解禁可能成为明显风险。相反，如果公司基本面强、机构持仓稳定、解禁比例可控，解禁反而可能提供流动性改善和二次买入机会。

## 指数纳入和港股通

报告还关注 MSCI、恒生指数以及港股通纳入带来的资金流。符合条件的港股新上市公司可能被纳入主要指数或港股通。指数纳入和南向资金准入可以在一定程度上对冲 IPO 供给、再融资和解禁压力。对投资者而言，筛选 IPO 后机会不能只看公司本身，还要看是否具备进入指数、进入港股通、吸引南向资金和全球被动资金的条件。

## 投资框架

高盛的框架可以转化为一个实用清单：选择行业景气度高、成长路径清晰、发行估值合理、散户认购热度强但不过度泡沫化、基石比例适中、自由流通结构健康、解禁压力可控、且未来可能纳入指数或港股通的公司。回避上市后涨幅巨大、解禁比例高、基本面不确定、流动性差或依赖短期情绪的新股。

总体结论是，香港 IPO 市场的机会正在增加，但这是一种结构性 alpha，不是无风险 beta。最好的策略是把 IPO 当作“一级发行结构加二级市场流动性加行业成长”的组合问题来分析，而不是只看认购倍数或首日涨幅。
