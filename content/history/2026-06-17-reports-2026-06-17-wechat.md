## Conclusion

Goldman Sachs argues that the reported RMB 2 trillion Chinese data-center investment plan is not genuinely new information. It appears to be part of the broader “Six Networks” infrastructure framework already mentioned around the Two Sessions and likely to be embedded in the 15th Five-Year Plan. The more important investment conclusion is that China’s government-led infrastructure mix is shifting from traditional concrete-heavy spending toward strategic infrastructure: computing power, data centers, communications, power grids, logistics, water systems and urban underground pipelines.

## Key Data and Arguments

The “Six Networks” are water, new-type power grids, computing power/data centers, next-generation communications, urban underground pipelines and logistics. Funding is expected to come from a mix of central and local government bonds, policy-bank financing tools and commercial bank credit. This matters because it changes the direction of fiscal support: the marginal stimulus is increasingly tied to AI infrastructure, high-tech manufacturing, strategic supply chains and livelihood-related urban systems.

The most important quantitative point is power demand. Goldman estimates China data-center electricity consumption may rise from about 170 TWh in 2025 to 800 TWh in 2030. That implies roughly 36% annualized growth and would lift data centers from about 1.6% of total national electricity consumption to around 6%. This is not just an internet or cloud topic; it directly affects power grids, generation mix, cooling equipment, land use, chip supply, optical networks and industrial policy.

## Investment Implications

The report is positive for China AI infrastructure, data centers, power equipment, grid operators, optical/communications supply chains and selected cloud platforms. It is less useful as a one-day “new-news” trade because the headline appears to repackage an existing policy direction. The better approach is to track actual bond issuance, local project approvals, policy-bank tools, grid connection data and hyperscaler/cloud capex. The risk is that official ambition does not translate into high-return private projects, especially if utilization, pricing or power constraints lag capacity construction.

## Conclusion

The report lays out how Asian central banks can respond to an external shock that weakens currencies, raises imported inflation and hurts growth at the same time. The key message is that policy rates are only one part of the toolkit. In Asia, central banks often combine spot and forward FX intervention, front-end money-market management, macroprudential measures and selective capital-flow tools before resorting to broader rate hikes.

## Framework

Goldman separates shocks by source and persistence. If the pressure is mainly a temporary capital-flow shock, FX intervention and liquidity tools are more appropriate. If it reflects a structural current-account problem or persistent inflation shock, central banks eventually need tighter monetary policy or stronger flow measures. The framework also distinguishes current-account stress from capital-account stress, because the correct response differs.

## Policy Toolkit

The toolkit includes direct spot FX intervention, indirect intervention through state banks or public-sector entities, forward-market management, FX-swap operations, front-end rate control, capital outflow limits and inflow incentives. Managing the forward curve can be especially useful when depreciation pressure is concentrated in hedging demand or short-term capital movement. Front-end tightening can raise the cost of speculative short positions without immediately signaling a full policy-rate hiking cycle.

## Country Lessons

Indonesia is used as an example of escalation: first intervention, then tighter front-end liquidity, then rate hikes when pressure persisted. The Philippines also used rate hikes when inflation and currency pressure became more entrenched. India has historically preferred a mix of reserves, inflow encouragement and macroprudential tools. Korea tends to manage both outflows and inflows given its open capital account and large institutional-investor overseas allocation.

## Investment Implications

For investors, the report argues against reading every Asian FX defense as an immediate rate-hike signal. Currencies with large reserve buffers and credible policy institutions may stabilize through intervention first. However, if oil or imported inflation pressure persists, duration markets can reprice hawkishly and high-beta FX may remain vulnerable. The key indicators are reserve drawdown, forwards, front-end rates, import cover, inflation expectations and central-bank communication.

## Conclusion

Goldman remains constructive on credit yield demand but expects volatility to normalize from unusually compressed levels. The macro backdrop has improved versus earlier fears because US activity data have been resilient, but the higher Treasury-yield floor and geopolitical commodity risk mean spread volatility should not stay as cheap as it has been. The preferred expression is buying credit volatility, especially in 1-month and 3-month tenors.

## Key Arguments

The report notes that stronger US data reduced the risk of a severe growth slowdown. Goldman’s Fed view also shifted: the final two rate cuts are now expected only in June and December 2027. This higher-for-longer path changes the corporate-credit debate. On one hand, higher risk-free yields support demand from yield-based buyers. On the other hand, they also leave less room for valuation error and can keep macro volatility elevated.

Goldman still sees some corporate spread widening by year-end, but less than previously forecast in March. The reason is that resilient activity lowers the growth-risk premium and continued demand for all-in yield supports credit. EUR credit spread widening forecasts were also moderated.

## Risk and Trade Advice

The report highlights left-tail commodity disruption as the main risk: an energy shock can hurt growth, inflation and margins simultaneously. Because implied credit volatility is low relative to this risk distribution, buying credit vol is presented as an attractive hedge and relative-value position. Short-dated 1-month and 3-month tenors are preferred because they can benefit if the market moves from calm carry-seeking behavior to more event-driven spread repricing.

## Investment Implications

The advice is not to abandon credit, but to avoid being complacent about volatility. Carry can still work if growth holds, but portfolios should include convexity against normalization in spread volatility. Credit investors should track oil, earnings revisions, refinancing conditions, fallen-angel risk and fund flows, rather than relying only on headline spreads.

## Conclusion

Goldman sees the rapid rise of fresh-snack retail chains in China as evidence that snack demand is still resilient despite macro concerns. The report’s main read-across is to value retailers such as Busy Ming and Wanchen: fresh-snack chains validate consumer appetite for value, freshness, fast turnover and experiential store formats, but they also increase competitive pressure and force incumbents to upgrade categories and formats.

Since 2025, more than 10 fresh-snack brands and over 200 stores have reportedly emerged. The report is positive on retailers that can upgrade store formats while controlling procurement, wastage and labor. Investors should track daily sales per store, gross margin, spoilage/wastage, franchise economics, repeat purchase, geographic expansion and whether new fresh-snack brands are eroding incumbents’ traffic. The report favors scenario thinking over a one-direction oil call. Energy equities, tankers, inflation hedges and commodity-linked FX benefit in adverse/severe outcomes, while airlines, consumers and importers benefit if the benign scenario unfolds. ## Commodity Analyst - El Nino and Agricultural Commodities

# Commodity Analyst - El Nino and Agricultural Commodities

Batch: WeChat File Transfer Summary 2026-06-17
Original index: 015

Extraction audit:

- Pages: 8
- Extracted characters: 26937

## Conclusion

The report argues that El Nino is not automatically globally food-inflationary. Its impact is geographically uneven: some crop regions suffer drought while others receive favorable rainfall, and global crop outcomes often offset. The biggest risk is not broad global food shortages but concentrated stress in soft commodities and policy reactions such as export bans.

## Key Data

NOAA indicates El Nino conditions are present, with a 63% probability that the 2026-27 winter becomes very strong or “super” El Nino. Strong or very strong El Nino events have occurred only eight times in the past 75 years, so the event deserves attention even if the price impact is uncertain.

El Nino has weaker correlation with global temperatures than with rainfall patterns. It tends to bring drier conditions to India, Southeast Asia, Australia, Southern Africa and northern South America, while making other regions wetter. This asymmetry matters because crop outcomes depend on local timing, soil moisture, planting cycles and irrigation.

## Commodity Implications

The report sees more risk in concentrated soft commodities, especially palm oil, because Southeast Asian drought can hit a large share of global supply. Rice and sugar may be affected less by direct global crop loss than by precautionary policy actions. India’s 2023 rice export ban is a clear example of how governments can amplify market stress even when physical supply loss is not catastrophic. India has also introduced sugar export restrictions.

## Investment Advice

Investors should not blindly buy all agriculture commodities on El Nino headlines. The better approach is to track regional rainfall anomalies, crop calendars, export-policy announcements, palm oil yields, Indian monsoon performance and inventories. The main tradable risks are palm oil, rice-policy premium, sugar-policy premium and potentially fertilizer/agri-input sentiment.
