## 01. Global Rates Trader - Relaxed, Not Relieved

Goldman's global rates strategy note argues that last week's stabilization in long-end yields should be read as a pause, not an all-clear. US, UK, and Japanese long bonds remain under pressure from fundamentals, supply, fiscal uncertainty, and higher required term premium. The move above `4.5%` in 10-year Treasuries and above `5.0%` in 30-year Treasuries was partly amplified by convexity dynamics, MBS duration extension, and options gamma, but the underlying driver is still a market demanding more compensation to hold duration.

For the US, Goldman says long-end Treasuries are now slightly cheap to fair, but not cheap enough to justify a deeper rally without a macro catalyst. The selloff since early May may have implied roughly `USD 40bn` of 10-year-equivalent duration extension for MBS held by traditional hedgers. However, the actual hedging flow is uncertain because GSE retained portfolios have grown sharply, with FNMA more than doubling and FHLMC rising about `50%` over the 12 months through March, while their behavior suggests greater tolerance for duration gaps.

Funding conditions are also changing. Earlier, negative bill issuance, Fed reserve management purchases, and a drawdown in the Treasury General Account had supported liquidity and lowered funding costs. Those effects are reversing as bill issuance turns positive, RMPs slow, and the TGA is likely rebuilt toward Treasury's target. Goldman's baseline is that RMPs may slow further to an average of `USD 5bn` per month until early 2027, taking steady-state reserves toward roughly `11%` of bank assets, versus `11.5%-12.0%` recently.

Europe remains the preferred relative duration market. Goldman expects European duration to stay better protected because the ECB has acted more proactively. Even if a prolonged Hormuz disruption pushes European rates higher, the move would likely be front-end led, with curves flattening or inverting. The report continues to recommend being long `5y5y EUR real yields`. European sovereign credit may have less room to tighten, but still offers reasonable carry and should be more insulated from front-end rate volatility.

The UK offers an improving valuation case, especially in 10-year Gilts and 5y5y rates, but political risks mean compression in the Gilt risk premium is likely to be gradual. Japan's long-end JGB moderation mainly reflects sensitivity to supply-demand dynamics, but Goldman still argues that without inflation moderation or fiscal restraint, policy rate outcomes will need to do more work to anchor the long end. Australia is another market where Goldman thinks hawkish risks are underpriced, with only about `19bp` of hike risk priced through September.

The forecast table is important. Goldman sees the US 10-year yield moving from spot `4.57%` to `4.30%` in 2Q26, `4.20%` in 3Q26, `4.10%` in 4Q26, and `4.15%` by 4Q27. The UK 10-year is forecast to move from `4.91%` to `4.25%` by 4Q27. Japan's 10-year is forecast to fall from `2.76%` to `2.25%` by 4Q27. This is not a structurally bearish bond view, but it is a warning that the market needs a genuine macro shift, not just a short-term oil pullback, for yields to fall sustainably.

Investment takeaway: do not chase a broad long-duration rally simply because yields stabilized. The cleaner relative trades are in European real rates and selective UK duration value. A real Treasury rally likely requires lower energy risk, weaker inflation data, softer growth, or stress in risk assets. The key risk to this view is a rapid Hormuz de-escalation and a sharp oil decline, which could create a tactical bond rally. The opposite risk is that fiscal supply, AI credit issuance, and global long-bond supply keep long-term funding rates elevated.

## 02. Hedge Fund Trend Monitor - All In on AI

This report analyzes `1,059` hedge funds with `USD 4.6tn` of gross equity positions at the start of 2Q26: `USD 3.1tn` long and `USD 1.5tn` short. The conclusion is direct: hedge funds entered 2Q26 doubling down on the AI trade. Their net tilt to Information Technology rose by `+853bp`, the largest quarterly increase to the sector on record.

Performance has been solid but highly concentrated. US equity long-short hedge funds returned about `+7%` year to date through May 21. The Hedge Fund VIP basket returned `+13%`, the equal-weight S&P 500 returned `+7%`, and the most concentrated short basket rallied `+27%`. This means hedge funds have benefited from market beta and technology long alpha, but they have also faced a broad short squeeze. Momentum has been a major tailwind: Goldman's equal-weight long-short Momentum factor is up about `30%` YTD, and hedge fund long portfolios carry Momentum exposure in the `90th percentile` since 2001.

Leverage remains elevated. GS Prime Services data show fundamental long-short hedge fund net and gross leverage at the `85th` and `94th` percentiles versus the last five years. Overall long-short gross leverage is roughly `321%`, and fundamental long-short gross leverage roughly `212%`. Median S&P 500 short interest has risen to `3%` of market cap, the highest level since 2011. Defensive sectors are the most extreme relative to history: Health Care short interest is `2.7%` of market cap and at the `100th percentile`; Utilities are `3.1%` and `99th percentile`; Consumer Staples are `3.7%` and `98th percentile`.

Portfolio concentration is also high. The typical hedge fund holds `72%` of its long portfolio in its top 10 positions. Average quarterly turnover rose to `28%` of distinct equity positions and `16%` of the largest positions, the highest since 2021. Information Technology turnover was `31%`, a 10-year high. This suggests managers are not just holding static tech exposure; they are actively rotating within technology.

The most important rotation is from software to semiconductors and AI infrastructure. Hedge funds now hold a record `10%` weight in Semiconductors in their long portfolios, while Software has fallen to `6%`, the lowest since 2019. The average stock in the GS AI Semiconductor basket is now held by roughly `10%` of hedge funds. Ownership also rose across Data Centers, Inference, Optical Networking, Liquid Cooling, and Power-related AI baskets.

AI-related names with large net increases in hedge fund popularity include `META`, `SSNC`, `LRCX`, `AMAT`, `ASML`, `NRG`, `ADI`, `NOK`, `INTC`, and `DOCU`. Names with large decreases include `VRT`, `MSFT`, `WDC`, `ETN`, and `CARR`. This is not a rejection of AI. It is a rotation away from some crowded winners and toward semicap equipment, memory, inference, optical networking, and power.

The Hedge Fund VIP list is still dominated by mega-cap tech. `AMZN` remains the most popular hedge fund long for the 10th consecutive quarter. The top five are `AMZN`, `NVDA`, `GOOGL`, `MSFT`, and `META`, followed by `TSM`, `AAPL`, `AVGO`, and `MU`. The VIP basket has outperformed the S&P 500 in `59%` of quarters since 2001 with an average quarterly excess return of `53bp`. New VIP constituents include `BE`, `COHR`, `CTRI`, `HUT`, `LRCX`, `MRVL`, `MTZ`, `RVMD`, `SPGI`, `UNH`, and `VST`.

The Rising Stars list is especially relevant. `SNDK` gained `46` hedge fund owners and is up `462%` YTD. `LRCX` gained `34`, `AMAT` `32`, `RVMD` `32`, `ADI` `29`, `WFC` `27`, and `MU` `24`. Large-cap names with high short interest include `CRWV` at `14%` of market cap, `KMB` at `13%`, `WDAY` at `13%`, `MSTR` at `11%`, `CVNA` at `11%`, and `COIN` at `11%`.

ETF usage is rising too. ETFs now account for `4.9%` of hedge fund long portfolios, the highest since the GFC. ETF longs equal `35%` of gross hedge fund ETF exposure, a record. `SPY` and `IVV` together represent roughly half of total hedge fund long ETF ownership and about one-third of total gross ETF ownership.

Investment takeaway: the most important institutional trade is still AI, but the crowding risk is high. The best areas are not broad AI labels, but bottleneck segments where hedge fund ownership is rising and fundamentals are tangible: semicap equipment, memory, AI data centers, inference, optical networking, power, and selected financials. The main risk is a crowded Momentum unwind if higher oil, higher rates, or weaker risk appetite triggers deleveraging.

## 03. US Weekly Kickstart - Funds Continue Rotating From Software To Semiconductors

This Goldman report combines hedge fund and mutual fund positioning data, covering roughly `USD 9tn` of equity positions at the start of 2Q26: `1,059` hedge funds with `USD 4.6tn` of gross exposure and `509` large-cap active mutual funds with `USD 3.9tn` in assets. The central conclusion is that both hedge funds and mutual funds are continuing to rotate from Software toward Semiconductors.

Performance shows why active managers are struggling. US equity long-short hedge funds are up about `+7%` YTD. The S&P 500 is up `+9%`, the Nasdaq 100 `+17%`, Russell 2000 `+15%`, and the most concentrated short basket `+27%`. Large-cap core mutual funds are also up `+7%`, but only `30%` have outperformed their benchmarks, below the historical average of `37%` since 2007. Large-cap growth funds have a `32%` outperformance rate, large-cap value `34%`, and large-cap core only `26%`. The market is narrow, and managers who are underweight AI/Momentum have struggled.

Hedge funds initially reduced exposure during March geopolitical stress, but then raised net leverage back to the `85th percentile` of the last five years. Mutual funds increased cash from a record low of `1.1%` at the start of 2026 to `1.4%` at the start of April, but cash is still low. Sector positioning is broadly aligned: both hedge funds and mutual funds are overweight Industrials and underweight Information Technology. The major differences are Financials, where mutual funds are overweight and hedge funds underweight, and Consumer Discretionary, where hedge funds are overweight and mutual funds underweight.

The software-to-semis rotation is the core message. Mutual funds entered 2Q with their lowest Software exposure since at least 2012. Excluding mega-caps, mutual fund Semis versus Software tilt is the highest since 2012. Hedge funds entered 2Q with Software at its smallest long-portfolio weight since 2019 and Semiconductors at a record high. `MSFT` was among the largest net-share reductions for both hedge funds and mutual funds. Mutual funds reduced ownership in all other Magnificent 7 names, while hedge funds reduced most of the group but increased `META` and `AAPL`. Within Semis, hedge funds added to `LRCX`, `AMAT`, and `ASML`; mutual funds added to `INTC` and `SITM`.

There are four shared favorites this quarter: `BA`, `MA`, `MRVL`, and `V`. These are constituents of both the Hedge Fund VIP basket and the Mutual Fund Overweight basket. `MRVL` entered the shared favorites list, while `C` and `VRT` dropped out. Every Magnificent 7 stock is a hedge fund VIP but also a mutual fund underweight, which reflects either valuation caution or benchmark constraints. Shared favorites are up `+10%` YTD, outperforming the equal-weight S&P 500 by `3pp`. Since 2013, shared favorites have delivered a `16%` annual return with `22%` standard deviation. Their median P/E is `34x`, well above the S&P 500 median of `18x`.

The earnings season remains supportive, especially for large technology. As of May 22, `474` S&P 500 companies had reported 1Q26 results. On an equal-weighted basis, `64%` delivered positive earnings surprises and `82%` delivered absolute positive earnings surprises; `52%` delivered positive revenue surprises. On a cap-weighted basis, the numbers are stronger: S&P 500 earnings surprise rate `78%`, revenue surprise rate `74%`. Communication Services and Information Technology are the strongest sectors, with cap-weighted earnings positive surprise rates of `96%` and `95%`.

Goldman's top-down S&P 500 EPS forecasts are `USD 275` in 2025, `USD 309` in 2026, and `USD 342` in 2027. The year-end 2026 S&P 500 target is `7,600`, implying about `2%` upside from current levels. Current next-12-month P/E is around `23x`.

Investment takeaway: the US equity index is not broadly cheap and index upside is limited, but alpha remains in AI hardware and semiconductors. Software faces valuation pressure, questions about AI substitution, and uncertainty over incremental revenue. Semiconductors face crowding and cycle risks. The best opportunities are likely in companies where institutional ownership is rising, earnings are being revised upward, orders are visible, and supply bottlenecks remain real.

## 04. MoneyWeek - 22 May 2026

This issue of MoneyWeek is framed around inflation shock, AI enthusiasm, and real-asset investing. The editor's letter argues that stock markets may be underestimating the persistence of the Hormuz/energy shock. The editor compares current market complacency with 2007 before the credit crisis and February 2020 before Covid. The recommendation is to avoid bonds, especially UK bonds, and favor income, raw materials, and energy. Commodity ETFs and industrial metals, especially copper, are presented as ways to benefit from electrification and AI data-center demand, while years of underinvestment have constrained mine supply.

The emerging-market section argues that EM equities are strong but increasingly concentrated in technology. MSCI EM is up `16%` in 2026 and recently hit an all-time high. Brazil is up `8.5%`, helped by commodities and election hopes. Korea's Kospi is up `67%` this year despite Korea importing about `70%` of its crude oil from the Middle East. But the real driver is AI and chips. Information Technology is `36.8%` of MSCI EM, higher than `27.6%` in developed markets. Materials and Energy are only `10.5%` of MSCI EM, not dramatically higher than `7.7%` in developed markets. EM has therefore become less of a diversification asset and more exposed to the same AI concentration risk. The Indian rupee, Indonesian rupiah, and Philippine peso hitting record lows are early signs of energy stress.

Japan is treated as a mixed case. The Nikkei 225 is up `15%` this year, while Topix is up `9%`, and the gap between the two is at a high in records going back to 1970. This implies a narrow large-cap tech-led rally, while the broader market may still be more reasonably valued. Japan's 10-year yield is at the highest level since 1996. With gross public debt around `206%` of GDP in 2024 and Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi preparing a supplementary budget to offset Hormuz-related costs, Japan may add to global bond supply pressure. Still, corporate governance reforms and higher capital efficiency remain medium-term positives.

The AI section focuses on Cerebras. Cerebras shares jumped `68%` on the first trading day, raising `USD 5.5bn`, the largest IPO of the year and the largest US semiconductor listing in history. Its market value reached `USD 67bn`. MoneyWeek acknowledges the value of wafer-scale chips for certain AI workloads, but warns that Cerebras is still a small, unprofitable, cash-burning company competing against Nvidia. OpenAI's latest fundraising values it at `USD 850bn`, up from `USD 350bn` only a few months earlier. This shows that AI valuation enthusiasm remains intense. The reported NextEra Energy-Dominion Energy merger is also interpreted as evidence that AI data-center power demand is reshaping the utility industry, especially in Northern Virginia's data-center corridor.

The US-China article interprets the Beijing summit as a stabilizing stalemate, not a structural reset. Trump spent `43 hours` in Beijing, giving Xi the optics of a G2-style meeting. The reported deal included China buying `200` Boeing jets and at least `USD 17bn` of US agricultural goods through 2028, but this fell short of investor hopes for `500` jets. The article argues that US policy remains competitive in AI, semiconductors, biotech, critical minerals, ports, the Arctic, and digital infrastructure. Still, both economies remain deeply interdependent, and a trade truce serves both sides' domestic political needs.

The Kazakhstan feature is the most investable article. It argues that Kazakhstan could become a key Western alternative to Chinese critical-mineral dominance. Key data: China controls `60%` of global rare-earth mining, `91%` of refining, and `94%` of magnet production. After China's April 2025 export restrictions, rare-earth magnet shipments to the US fell `93%` YoY by May. China controls `79%` of global tungsten supply, and ammonium paratungstate prices have risen `716%` in a year. Russia controls `46%` of global uranium enrichment. Kazakhstan produces `40%` of the world's uranium and `18%` of the titanium used in global aerospace. It is also a top-ten producer of copper, gold, chromium, beryllium, niobium, tantalum, and tungsten, may become the second-largest gallium producer, and has licensed only `16%` of its available exploration territory. The Middle Corridor has cut Kazakhstan-Europe transit time from `50 days` in 2023 to `18 days`, with a `10-day` target by 2030.

Potential Kazakhstan exposures mentioned include `Kaspi.kz (KSPI)`, `Halyk Bank (HSBK)`, `Kazatomprom (KAP)`, `KazMunaiGaz`, `East Star Resources (EST)`, and `Air Astana (AIRA)`. The key risk is resource nationalism. New oil, gas, and uranium projects may require at least `50%` state-owned participation, and one uranium license change would have handed Kazatomprom a `75%` stake. The government has also launched a `USD 100bn` arbitration claim against Kashagan and Karachaganak oil projects.

The cover story is about healthspan, not lifespan. The argument is that aging populations create a medical-cost crisis because people often spend their final `15` years with multiple chronic conditions, and healthcare spending rises almost vertically after age 75. Opportunities include cardiovascular devices and drugs, GLP-1s, Alzheimer's diagnostics and treatments, deep-sleep medicine, immune aging, vaccines, and wearables. Suggested companies include `Eli Lilly`, `Novo Nordisk`, `NewAmsterdam Pharma`, `Cytokinetics`, `Boston Scientific`, and `PacBio`. Notable data: a 2024 study found Shingrix increased dementia-free lifespan by about `17%`; a 2025 study found that Arexvy reduced dementia diagnoses over the next 18 months by about one-third among people over 60. Eli Lilly revenue has more than doubled since 2020 and is expected to grow around `15%` per year, trading at `23x 2027E` earnings. Novo Nordisk trades at `14.2x 2027E` with a `3.8%` yield. Boston Scientific gets about two-thirds of revenue from cardiovascular devices, has more than doubled sales since 2020, and trades at about `14x 2027E`.

The investment trust article argues that the UK investment trust discount recovery is still early. Average discounts excluding 3i narrowed from `15%` to `12.5%` in 2026, while the FTSE All-Share Closed End Investments sector returned `16.1%`. But total assets fell `GBP 3bn` to `GBP 265.5bn` because buybacks of `GBP 10.2bn` and mergers/liquidations of `GBP 9.5bn` exceeded only `GBP 530mn` of new fundraising. This year, average discounts have narrowed to `11.1%`, and the sector returned `4.7%` in the first four months, with `88%` of funds delivering positive share-price total returns. Equity trust activist opportunities may be reduced, but alternatives still have discount-recovery potential.

Ceres Power is presented as an AI-power infrastructure stock. The shares are up `240%` YTD and `850%` over 12 months, but still about `50%` below their 2021 high. Data-center electricity demand rose `17%` in 2025. Average newbuild data-center power demand is expected to rise from `47MW` in 2025 to nearly `110MW` by 2030. Ceres' solid oxide fuel cells can run on natural gas, biogas, or hydrogen blends and can be delivered and turned on in months rather than years. But valuation risk is high: 2028 revenue is expected around `GBP 80mn` against a `GBP 1.4bn` market cap, and EBITDA is projected to reach only `GBP 7.8mn` by December 2028. Another clear trade idea is to short `York Space Systems (YSS)` at `USD 24.02`, because the company depends heavily on the US Space Development Agency and trades at nearly `8x sales` despite severe customer and profitability risks.

Investment takeaway: MoneyWeek favors real assets, energy, industrial metals, critical minerals, AI power infrastructure, and high-quality income equities, while warning against long-duration bonds and speculative AI IPOs. The core macro risk is an energy shock that creates sticky inflation and slower real demand at the same time.

## 05. Asia Economics Analyst - RMB Internationalization Beyond Trade Settlement

Goldman's main argument is that RMB internationalization has improved, but remains far below China's global economic and trade footprint. The next stage is not just trade settlement. It requires deeper offshore RMB markets, better hedging tools, and a broader pool of RMB assets. The likely path is offshore-led and Hong Kong-centered, rather than rapid broad capital-account liberalization.

The current numbers show progress but also a large gap. RMB invoicing in goods imports among sample countries has roughly doubled since 2020, but was still only about `1.5%` in 2023. RMB's share of global payments has stayed around `3%-4%` over the past two years. RMB accounted for about `2%` of global official reserves and only `0.9%` of international debt securities by currency denomination in 2025. These shares are far below China's `19%` share of global GDP in 2024 and `12%` share of global trade in 2025. The clearest progress is China-linked trade settlement: RMB settlement in China's goods trade rose from `13%` in 2019 to `30%` in 2025.

Cross-border RMB use has become investment-driven. Total cross-border RMB transactions rose from `RMB 9tn` in 2017 to `RMB 64tn` in 2024. Capital and financial account transactions now account for roughly `75%` of the total. In 2024, bond investment accounted for `46%` of cross-border RMB payments, goods trade `19%`, direct investment `13%`, QFI `7%`, and Stock Connect `4%`. This means the next stage depends on whether foreign investors can fund, hedge, and hold RMB assets conveniently.

The report's Hong Kong framework is important. Long-term RMB liquidity comes from retail flows, trade, investment, and cross-border lending. Short-term official support comes from the `RMB 800bn` PBOC-HKMA standing swap line and HKMA liquidity facilities. Hong Kong's RMB liquidity pool includes around `RMB 1.1tn` of deposits and certificates of deposit. This liquidity then feeds offshore RMB lending, dim sum bonds, RMB derivatives, and onshore assets through Bond Connect and Stock Connect. A sustainable international currency market requires RMB to become easier to fund, easier to hedge, and more attractive to hold.

Offshore RMB liquidity has improved. In 2023-24, short-term offshore RMB funding in Hong Kong could be around `2pp` higher than onshore funding, nearly doubling the funding cost when onshore rates were around `2%`. Since April 2026, the spread between 3-month CNH HIBOR and 3-month SHIBOR has narrowed to about `15-25bp`, compared with `100-200bp` previously. Goldman forecasts `USD/CNY` at `6.50` in 12 months, supported by policy intention, export strength, and significant FX undervaluation. Continued RMB appreciation should help keep onshore-offshore funding gaps narrow and stable.

Policy support includes the February 2025 HKMA RMB Business Facility, the September 2025 cross-boundary bond repo program, and the February 2026 easing of cross-border RMB interbank financing rules. Offshore RMB clearing banks have expanded from the first in Hong Kong in 2003 to `35` clearing banks in `33` economies by June 2025. Economies covered by RMB clearing arrangements accounted for about `64%` of China's goods trade in 2025. PBOC swap line partners accounted for about `60%` of China's goods trade, but actual drawings remain low at around `2%-3%` of capacity.

Risk-management tools are the main bottleneck. RMB's share of global OTC FX derivatives turnover rose from `1.0%` in 2010 to `8.1%` in 2025, but RMB's share of global OTC interest-rate derivatives turnover was only `0.8%` in 2025. RMB FX derivatives are concentrated: `96%` trade against the USD, and FX swaps account for about `68%` of turnover. More than `90%` of offshore RMB trading takes place in Hong Kong, Singapore, the UK, and the US; Hong Kong alone accounts for more than `40%` of OTC RMB FX derivatives turnover outside mainland China.

FX hedging is relatively developed; long-duration rates hedging is not. Onshore CGB futures turnover in 2025 was concentrated in 30-year and 10-year contracts, at `37%` and `25%` of turnover. By contrast, FR007 IRS liquidity is mostly front-end and 5-year. CGB futures have historically been a better hedge for long-end CGBs than IRS or offshore NDIRS. In April 2026, qualified foreign investors were allowed to trade onshore CGB futures for hedging purposes, and Hong Kong is preparing offshore CGB futures.

The asset pool also needs to broaden. China's domestic markets are already large, with A-share market cap around `RMB 120tn` and bond custody balance close to `RMB 200tn`. But foreign participation has not risen mechanically. Foreign investors have reduced RMB bond holdings while increasing RMB equity holdings, and their overall share of domestic assets has declined. Dim sum bonds remain small, with only about `RMB 1.4tn` outstanding as of April 2026. More offshore RMB government bond issuance could help build a liquid yield curve. Connect programs could expand to REITs, private funds, and commodity futures.

Investment takeaway: the best RMB internationalization opportunities are not generic payment stories. They are in the Hong Kong offshore RMB ecosystem: RMB funding, dim sum bonds, FX and rates derivatives, RMB commodity products, Connect expansion, and trading/custody infrastructure. The risks are controlled capital-account opening, insufficient long-duration hedging tools, underdeveloped credit-risk tools, and renewed RMB depreciation pressure that could raise CNH funding costs.

## 06. XPeng - Robotaxi Call Takeaways, From L2 To L4, Buy

Goldman's XPeng note argues that XPeng is moving from L2+ consumer-vehicle autonomy toward L4 robotaxi capability. The key point is that XPeng is not trying to build a separate robotaxi-only company. It is using its passenger vehicle fleet, proprietary foundation model, in-house chips, compiler, simulation stack, and vehicle manufacturing capabilities to create a scalable physical-AI platform. Goldman maintains Buy on both ADR and H shares, with DCF-based 12-month target prices of `USD 22` and `HKD 85`, using `11.8%` WACC and `3.0%` terminal growth.

The event: XPeng's first mass-produced robotaxi based on the GX model rolled off the line on May 18, and road testing has started in Guangzhou. On May 20, XPeng launched its flagship six-seat SUV, the GX. The company said it received nearly `25,000` non-refundable orders within 12 hours. In Goldman's comparison of 16 six-seat SUV models launched since 2025, the GX, especially the EREV version, ranks among the top three because of its balanced price, range, and size.

Management highlighted three barriers to moving from L2 to L4: cross-region generalization, cross-model generalization, and continuous investment requirements. Historically, robotaxi scaling required extensive mapping, testing, and rule modifications for each operating domain. XPeng is trying to solve this through VLA2.0, a single foundation model that currently supports urban roads and is expected to expand to parks, campuses, and parking lots after the next OTA. Cross-model generalization is difficult because different vehicles have different sizes and features, but XPeng says the technology can extend beyond GX to all Ultra models.

XPeng has deliberately avoided L3 because management sees no clear commercial logic and unresolved liability issues. The company sees consumer vehicle L4 and robotaxi as using the same architecture and sensor stack, with robotaxi adding more redundancy. GX already has "one box" safety redundancy, and XPeng eventually aims to develop a steering-wheel-free one-box system that can return vehicles to repair facilities autonomously.

The technology stack is framed as physical AI. XPeng says its training infrastructure can double data volume and improve training efficiency by `70%`. Its data infrastructure benefits from the passenger vehicle fleet, which can upload large-scale selected data every day. Its in-house Turing architecture improved efficiency by `35%`, while proprietary chips and compilers delivered a `4-5x` efficiency gain on open-source models. Closed-loop simulation is critical because real-world testing alone is too slow; the company can test with regional datasets such as European scenarios.

The commercial strategy is to run GX trials this year and use later models for scaling once technical validation is complete. XPeng does not plan to build distinct models or do extensive pre-training for every city or country. Instead, it expects to use local NEV fleet data for corner-case training and post-training. Tesla FSD entering China is viewed positively because it will push domestic players toward product innovation, but XPeng argues that China's driving environment is significantly more complex than North America's.

Goldman's investment thesis is broader than robotaxi. XPeng has improved model launches since 4Q24, with Mona M03 and P7+ ranking among the top three in their segments. The company moved from only 1-2 new models per year during 2019-2023 to about `10` launches over 2024-2026, and now plans `10` new or refreshed models per year. Overseas expansion is accelerating, with `4` models in 2026 and localized production in Indonesia, Austria, and Malaysia.

Investment takeaway: XPeng's robotaxi strategy is best understood as an upward extension of consumer-vehicle autonomy, not as a standalone high-burn robotaxi fleet model. The key advantages are fleet data, end-to-end models, self-developed chips and compilers, vehicle cost control, and manufacturing speed. Near-term catalysts are GX/EREV ramp, overseas expansion, and margin improvement. Risks include weak sales, price competition, regulatory delays for L4, safety incidents, and softer demand.

## 07. USA - Governor Waller Supports Removing Easing Bias

This Goldman US economics note says the Fed is becoming more reluctant to cut and more willing to acknowledge future rate-hike risk. In a speech in Frankfurt, Governor Christopher Waller said he would support removing the implicit easing bias from the FOMC's post-meeting statement. He does not think the FOMC should hike in the near future, but said he can no longer rule out rate hikes further down the road if inflation does not abate soon, especially if inflation expectations show signs of becoming unanchored.

### A. Xu Guangqi, Matteo Ricci, and Knowledge Translation

The opening essay and long feature position Xu Guangqi as a rare late-Ming practical intellectual. He wrote about sweet potatoes, compiled agricultural works, translated Euclid's Elements, and led work on the Chongzhen calendar. He studied agriculture, mathematics, astronomy, military affairs, and water management. The article contrasts him with the literary scholar-official tradition and emphasizes his commitment to knowledge that could be applied in the world.

The feature begins with the Nanjing Museum VR exhibition of Matteo Ricci and Li Zhizao's world map. Ricci made a world map in Zhaoqing in 1584, drew another in Nanjing in 1599, and worked with Li Zhizao on the six-panel Kunyu Wanguo Quantu in Beijing in 1602. Ricci wanted Chinese literati to see more than exotic geography. He wanted to introduce latitude, meridians, the equator, tropics, polar regions, celestial spheres, and a different spatial order.

Xu met Ricci in Nanjing in 1600, starting a brief high point in Sino-Western knowledge exchange. The article places this meeting in global scientific time. In 1600, Kepler met Tycho Brahe in Prague. In 1601, Tycho died and Kepler obtained his data; Ricci entered Beijing in the same year. Kepler's calculations and Galileo's experiments helped launch the European scientific revolution. The Chongzhen calendar includes Copernicus, Tycho, and Kepler, but did not absorb Kepler's elliptical laws, showing the lag in translation and intellectual transfer.

Xu's life is linked to famine and practical governance. He was born in Shanghai in 1562 and died in Beijing in 1633. At the time, Shanghai county had about 100,000 households, 30,000-40,000 in the county town, and about `2,000` people engaged in textiles. Famine shaped his worldview. The article explains the old saying about famine every three years, decline every six, and devastation every twelve as different levels of agricultural disaster. Xu introduced sweet potatoes from Fujian into the Yangtze region and believed they could provide half a year's grain for local people.

The geography of Xujiahui matters. Xu's tomb is in Guangqi Park. In 1903, Shanghai Catholics restored the tomb for the 300th anniversary of Xu's baptism. In 1981, mathematician Su Buqing inscribed the tombstone. The Xujiahui Observatory was founded by late-Qing Jesuits in 1873 and operated for `77` years. It later became three institutions: Xujiahui Observatory, Sheshan Observatory, and Lujiahbang geomagnetic observatory. In 1879, Father Dechevrens analyzed a Shanghai typhoon; in 1884 the observatory established a Bund signal station. In 1923 it issued more than `600` weather warnings, including `146` in August during typhoon season.

The feature also discusses the Needham Question. Yang Zhenning argued that from 1600 to 1900 China resisted Western thought due to the lack of an independent middle class, an overly humanistic education system, the absence of natural philosophy, the civil-service exam system, and a weak tradition of logical deduction. Benjamin Elman rejected the hypothetical failure framing and argued for understanding Chinese science from within Chinese concepts of gezhi and evidential scholarship.

The interview with Li Tiangang is central. He says Song-Ming Neo-Confucianism moralized Chinese thought and lowered the knowledge demands placed on officials. Xu was different because he had geometric thinking and believed in determinate rules. Xu's translation of Euclid represented a system of concepts that could be deduced rather than merely observed case by case. Li describes Xu as a kind of technical official and the center of a Western learning group around calendar reform, artillery, agriculture, and military modernization. The exchange was interrupted by the Chinese Rites Controversy. In 1704, Rome banned Chinese Catholics from ancestor and Confucius rituals; in 1706, Kangxi responded by restricting missionary activity. Knowledge exchange gradually froze. Xu's famous idea, translated loosely, is: to surpass, first integrate; before integration, translate.

### C. Adult Intellectual-Disability Care Gap

The article about Beijing Huiling examines China's shortage of long-term care for adults with severe intellectual and developmental disabilities. Beijing Huiling operates community-style residential care sites. One site is a `160 sqm` three-bedroom apartment in Tiantongyuan that serves people aged 16 and above. He needed food cut into small pieces and assisted feeding. During the incident, he choked on small cakes purchased by the institution. Doctors removed three pieces from his airway. His parents sued, asking Beijing Huiling to assume `60%` responsibility and pay `RMB 1.2mn`. On March 30, 2026, the court found that the institution had not fully considered his condition and that the cake was an inducing factor. It also found possible delays in coordinating transfer during the outing. The court assigned `15%` responsibility and ordered compensation of just over `RMB 309,000`.

The article is not a simple blame story. Other parents still kept their children at Beijing Huiling because there are few alternatives. Many residents are in their 20s and 30s, cannot communicate normally, cannot care for themselves, and have aging parents who are physically and emotionally exhausted. Policy implication: the core issue is not one accident. It is the absence of a scalable public safety net for adults with severe disabilities.
